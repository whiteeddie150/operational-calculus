Tailboard Safety Briefing

Topic: Reduced Visibility, Rain, and Icy Road Conditions

Good morning team,

Today's focus is on driving hazards associated with rain, fog, and freezing
temperatures. These conditions reduce traction, limit visibility, and
increase stopping distance, making routine travel and emergency response
more dangerous.

Nationwide, adverse weather contributes to nearly 745,000 crashes each
year, resulting in more than 3,800 deaths and over 268,000 injuries. Most
of these incidents occur on wet pavement, and rainfall alone is involved in
nearly half of weather-related crashes. Ice and slush account for tens of
thousands more collisions and over 1,800 deaths annually. Fog adds another
layer of risk, contributing to more than 38,000 crashes each year due to
reduced visibility and delayed reaction times.

Sources include the Federal Highway Administration and National Highway
Traffic Safety Administration.

Here is what that means for us operationally.

Wet roads reduce tire traction, especially during the first rainfall when
oil residue rises to the surface. Standing water can cause hydroplaning and
loss of steering control. Near freezing temperatures, black ice forms first
on bridges, overpasses, and shaded roadways, and it is often invisible. Fog
and heavy rain reduce sight distance, making it harder to judge speed,
distance, and hazards ahead.

To manage these risks, adjust your driving to the conditions, not the
posted speed limit. Increase following distance to at least five to six
seconds on wet or icy roads. Use low-beam headlights in fog and rain to
improve visibility. Avoid sudden braking, acceleration, or sharp steering
inputs that can lead to loss of control. Slow down before bridges,
overpasses, and shaded areas where ice forms first.

If visibility drops below safe limits or road conditions deteriorate,
reassess travel. Delaying or rerouting is a safety decision, not a failure
to perform. Report hazardous conditions so others can adjust accordingly.

Bottom line: these hazards follow recognizable patterns, which means they
can be managed before they turn into a crash. That is the same principle
behind Gordon Graham's public-safety risk-management work on high-risk,
low-frequency events: predictable categories of loss can be controlled
ahead of time. Slow down, increase spacing, use proper lighting, and
prioritize arriving safely over arriving quickly. No task outweighs your
safety.
