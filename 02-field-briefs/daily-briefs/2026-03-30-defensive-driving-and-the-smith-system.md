Morning Safety Brief
Topic: Defensive Driving and the Smith System

Situation
Driving is one of the highest-risk activities we perform every day. Most
incidents don't happen because of one major mistake, they happen from a
series of small misses in awareness, spacing, and decision-making.

Why It Matters
Motor vehicle incidents remain a leading cause of occupational fatalities
in the United States. OSHA and NIOSH both identify defensive driving
practices as critical to reducing crashes, injuries, and downtime.
Using a structured system like the Smith System improves awareness,
reaction time, and overall control of the vehicle.

Operational Hazards
- Following too closely and reduced reaction time
- Limited visibility due to poor positioning
- Distractions inside or outside the vehicle
- Speed not adjusted for conditions
- Assuming other drivers will act predictably

Control Measures (Smith System – 5 Keys)
- Aim High in Steering: Look 15 seconds ahead
- Get the Big Picture: Maintain full awareness of surroundings
- Keep Your Eyes Moving: Avoid fixed focus and scan continuously
- Leave Yourself an Out: Maintain space and escape routes
- Make Sure They See You: Use signals, lights, and positioning

Relevant Federal Safety Guidance
NIOSH identifies motor vehicle incidents as a leading cause of work-related
deaths and emphasizes defensive driving and hazard awareness as key
prevention strategies.
https://www.cdc.gov/niosh/topics/motorvehicle

Supporting Language from PG&E Governing Documents
PG&E states that "nothing is more important to us than safety" and
emphasizes accountability in protecting employees, coworkers, and the
public through safe work practices.

Supporting Language from IBEW 1245 Physical Agreement
The Company and Union affirm that the safety of employees, contractors, and

Field Safety Reminder
Drive like no one sees you and always leave yourself a way out.

2-Sentence Safety Message
Defensive driving is about controlling space, visibility, and reaction
time. Use the Smith System and assume other drivers will make mistakes.

5 Quick Bullet Points
- Look ahead, not just at the vehicle in front of you
- Keep space around your vehicle
- Scan constantly, don't fixate
- Adjust speed for conditions
- Always leave yourself an escape route
