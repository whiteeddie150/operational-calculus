MORNING SAFETY BRIEF

Topic: Protect Your Attention

Supporting PG&E Virtue: Owners

Situation

Every day we make hundreds of decisions.

Most are routine.

Which gloves to wear.

Which tool to grab.

Where to park.

How to fuel equipment.

Routine is not the enemy.

In fact, routine is one of the ways experienced operators conserve mental
energy.

The challenge is knowing which decisions should become routine and which
ones should never become automatic.

Why It Matters

Some of the world's most successful leaders, including Steve Jobs and Mark
Zuckerberg, intentionally eliminated trivial daily decisions to preserve
their attention for decisions that mattered most.

Field work is no different.

Standardizing our routines allows us to devote more attention to changing
hazards, unexpected conditions, and the safety of the people around us.

The Science

Human factors research shows that attention is a limited resource.

Routine tasks can become automatic through repetition, reducing the mental
effort required to perform them.

This is beneficial because it preserves cognitive capacity for novel,
uncertain, or high-consequence situations.

The danger is not routine itself.

The danger is allowing our observations to become routine.

Operational Awareness

Today ask yourself:

• Which parts of my work should be automatic?
• Which parts require deliberate attention every time?
• Am I following a routine, or am I making assumptions?
• Has today's work changed in ways that my routine won't detect?

Questioning Attitude

PG&E's Questioning Attitude reminds us to challenge assumptions.

Routine should automate predictable work.

It should never replace observation.

Today's conditions deserve today's attention.

Supporting PG&E Virtue

Owners

Owners create consistent routines because consistency reduces unnecessary
mental effort.

They then invest that saved attention where it matters most:

Protecting people.

Recognizing hazards.

Making sound decisions.

Safety Reminder

Routine is a tool.

Awareness is a responsibility.

Use routine to free your attention, not replace it.

2-Sentence Safety Message

Build routines that eliminate unnecessary decisions, but never let routine
replace observation. Save your thinking for the moments when reality no
longer matches your expectations.

5 Quick Bullet Points

• Standardize routine tasks
• Stay alert to changing conditions
• Challenge assumptions
• Reserve attention for unexpected hazards
• Let routine support awareness, not replace it

Closing Thought

The best operators don't think harder about everything.

They think harder about the things that have changed.

SCIENCE & THOUGHT LEADER ADDENDUM

Daniel Kahneman – Thinking, Fast and Slow

Kahneman described two modes of thinking. Routine, familiar tasks are often
handled quickly and automatically, while unfamiliar or high-consequence
situations require slower, more deliberate reasoning.

Practical Takeaway:
Reserve deliberate thinking for situations where the unexpected appears.

---

Gary Klein – Recognition-Primed Decision Making

Klein found that experienced operators rely on pattern recognition for
routine decisions, allowing them to focus their attention on conditions
that do not fit their expectations.

Practical Takeaway:
Expertise reduces unnecessary thinking but increases attention to anomalies.

---

Human Factors Research

Research in aviation, medicine, emergency services, and high-reliability
organizations shows that standardization reduces unnecessary cognitive
workload and improves performance. However, overreliance on routine can
lead to missed hazards if observation stops.

Practical Takeaway:
Standardize the predictable. Investigate the unexpected.

---

Steve Jobs & Mark Zuckerberg

Both publicly described simplifying routine personal decisions, such as
clothing choices, to preserve attention for more consequential work. While
this reflects a practical philosophy rather than a scientific study, it
illustrates an important human performance principle: eliminate low-value
decisions so attention remains available for high-value ones.
