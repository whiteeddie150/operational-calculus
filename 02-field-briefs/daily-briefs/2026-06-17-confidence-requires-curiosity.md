MORNING SAFETY BRIEF

Topic: Confidence Requires Curiosity

Supporting PG&E Virtue: Curious

Situation

Many of us perform the same tasks, use the same equipment, drive the same
roads, and work around the same hazards day after day.

Experience is one of our greatest assets.

But experience creates a hidden challenge.

The more familiar something becomes, the easier it is to stop actively
evaluating it.

Why It Matters

Most workplace incidents do not occur because experienced workers suddenly
forget how to do their jobs.

They occur when conditions change and the change goes unnoticed.

The hazard may be new.

The equipment condition may be new.

The environment may be new.

The worker's assumptions may be old.

The Science

Research in human factors consistently shows that familiarity can reduce
active scrutiny and hazard recognition.

As work becomes routine, the brain conserves effort by relying on
expectations and previous experience.

This is normally efficient.

However, it can cause us to overlook subtle changes that would have
attracted our attention when the task was new.

In simple terms:

The environment changes.

Our expectations often do not.

Human Performance Perspective

Confidence is valuable.

Confidence allows us to work efficiently, make decisions, and perform
complex tasks.

Complacency is not confidence.

Complacency begins when confidence convinces us there is nothing left to
learn.

The safest operators remain curious.

They continue asking:

• What changed?
• What is different today?
• What am I assuming?
• What have I stopped looking for?

Operational Awareness

Before beginning work today, take a moment to look at your environment with
fresh eyes.

Ask yourself:

• Is the terrain the same as yesterday?
• Is the equipment operating normally?
• Have weather conditions changed?
• Am I rushing because the task feels familiar?
• What might I be overlooking because I have seen this before?

Questioning Attitude

PG&E's Questioning Attitude encourages us to prefer facts over assumptions.

Experience tells us what usually happens.

Questioning Attitude helps us identify what is happening today.

Both are necessary.

Supporting PG&E Virtue

Curious

Curiosity is one of the best defenses against complacency.

Curiosity keeps us learning.

Curiosity keeps us observing.

Curiosity helps us recognize change before change becomes a problem.

Safety Reminder

The most dangerous hazard is often not the one we have never seen.

It is the one we believe we already understand completely.

2-Sentence Safety Message

Confidence is earned through experience. Curiosity ensures that experience
continues to serve us rather than blind us to changing conditions.

5 Quick Bullet Points

• Experience is an asset, not a substitute for awareness
• Look for what is different, not just what is familiar
• Challenge assumptions before beginning work
• Stay engaged even during routine tasks
• Let curiosity support your Questioning Attitude

Closing Thought

Experience teaches us what to expect. Curiosity helps us recognize when
reality no longer matches those expectations.

SCIENCE AND THOUGHT LEADER ADDENDUM

James Reason (Human Error)

Reason's work demonstrated that incidents rarely result from a single
mistake. They emerge when multiple weaknesses align. One recurring weakness
is the tendency to normalize conditions that have not previously produced
negative outcomes. Repeated success can create a false perception that risk
has been reduced when it has merely gone unpunished.

Practical Takeaway:
Success does not always validate the process.

---

Sidney Dekker (Drift Into Failure)

Dekker argues that people rarely choose unsafe work intentionally. Instead,
they adapt to conditions, shortcuts, and workarounds that have proven
successful in the past.

The result is a gradual drift where risk remains constant while perception
of risk declines.

Practical Takeaway:
Familiarity can make yesterday's exception become today's normal.

---

Karl Weick (Sensemaking)

Weick's research suggests that effective operators continuously update
their understanding of reality.

The danger is not lacking knowledge.

The danger is failing to notice when reality has changed.

Practical Takeaway:
The safest workers continuously compare expectations to current conditions.

---

NIOSH Hazard Recognition Research

NIOSH-supported research has repeatedly demonstrated that workers can
overlook hazards that are directly visible, particularly in familiar
environments.

Hazard recognition is not solely a knowledge problem.

It is also an attention problem.

Practical Takeaway:
Seeing and noticing are not the same thing.
