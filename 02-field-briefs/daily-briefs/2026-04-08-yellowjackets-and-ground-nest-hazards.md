Morning Safety Brief
Topic: Yellowjackets and Ground Nest Hazards

Situation
Crews working around vegetation, pole bases, guy wires, and underground
enclosures may encounter yellowjackets nesting in the ground. These nests
are often hidden and are typically discovered only after they are disturbed.

Why It Matters
Yellowjackets are responsible for a large portion of insect stings in the
U.S. and can attack in groups within seconds. Unlike bees, they can sting
multiple times and release alarm pheromones that trigger additional
attackers.
Stings from bees, wasps, and hornets result in over 200,000 emergency
department visits annually, and about 60–70 deaths per year in the U.S.,
primarily from allergic reactions (CDC; NIOSH).

Operational Hazards
- Hidden ground nests near work areas
- Disturbance from cutting, digging, or vibration
- Rapid swarm response after initial contact
- Multiple stings in seconds
- Reduced visibility of insect activity until close proximity

Control Measures
- Scan for insect activity before starting work
- Look and listen for low flight patterns and buzzing near ground openings
- Stop work immediately if activity increases
- Move away quickly and put distance between you and the nest
- Notify supervision and secure the area if a nest is identified

Relevant Federal Safety Guidance
NIOSH identifies insect stings as a workplace hazard and emphasizes
avoidance and awareness as primary controls.
https://www.cdc.gov/niosh/topics/outdoor

CDC guidance confirms that stinging insect incidents are common and can
result in serious medical emergencies.
https://www.cdc.gov/niosh/topics/insects

Supporting Language from PG&E Governing Documents
PG&E recognizes animals and insects as a workplace hazard category and
requires hazard identification and control before work begins.

Supporting Language from IBEW 1245 Physical Agreement
The Company and Union affirm that the safety of employees, contractors, and
the public is the highest priority and that hazard control is a shared
responsibility.

Immediate Actions (If Stung or Swarmed)
- Leave the area immediately and create distance
- Do not stop near the nest, keep moving
- Remove yourself and others from the hazard zone
- Seek medical evaluation for multiple stings
- Monitor for allergic reaction (difficulty breathing, swelling,
dizziness)

What NOT to Do
- Do not swat aggressively or stand your ground
- Do not attempt to locate or disturb the nest further
- Do not ignore symptoms after multiple stings

Potential Effects
- Single sting: pain, redness, localized swelling
- Multiple stings: systemic symptoms (nausea, dizziness, fatigue)
- Severe reaction: anaphylaxis, which can occur within minutes
- High sting counts can lead to toxic effects even without allergy

Field Safety Reminder
If you disturb a nest, your priority is distance. You will not outwork a
swarm.

2-Sentence Safety Message
Yellowjackets can attack in groups within seconds when a nest is disturbed.
If activity increases, stop work immediately and move away.

5 Quick Bullet Points
- Scan for insect activity before starting work
- Stop work if activity increases
- Move away quickly, do not swat
- Watch for hidden ground nests
- Report and secure the area
