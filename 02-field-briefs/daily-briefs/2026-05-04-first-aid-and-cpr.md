Morning Safety Brief
Topic: First Aid & CPR – First Few Minutes Matter

Situation
Medical emergencies can happen anywhere, not just on high-risk jobs. When
they do, the first few minutes before EMS arrives are critical.

Why It Matters
Early action saves lives. Immediate first aid and CPR can keep someone
alive long enough for higher-level care to take over.

Common Emergencies
- Cardiac arrest (unresponsive, not breathing normally)
- Severe bleeding
- Choking
- Heat illness or medical collapse

Operational Hazards
- Delay in recognizing the emergency
- Hesitation to act
- Unclear roles or confusion
- Doing nothing while waiting for help

Immediate Actions (General)
- Ensure the scene is safe
- Call 911 or direct someone to call
- Get an AED if available
- Stay with the patient and act

CPR Basics
- Check for responsiveness and normal breathing
- If not breathing normally, begin compressions
- Push hard and fast in the center of the chest
- Rate: 100–120 compressions per minute
- Depth: about 2 inches
- Allow full recoil between compressions
- Minimize interruptions

AED Use
- Turn it on and follow voice prompts
- Apply pads as shown
- Stand clear when advised
- Resume compressions immediately after shock or prompt

Severe Bleeding
- Apply direct pressure immediately
- Use a clean cloth, bandage, or gloved hand
- If bleeding continues, apply more pressure
- Use a tourniquet if trained and bleeding is uncontrolled

Choking
- If the person can't speak or breathe, act immediately
- Deliver abdominal thrusts until the airway clears
- If unresponsive, begin CPR

What NOT to Do
- Don't delay action waiting for someone else
- Don't stop compressions unless necessary
- Don't attempt advanced medical procedures
- Don't leave the patient alone

Follow-Up
- Transfer care to EMS when they arrive
- Report the incident per company guidance
- Replace used medical supplies

Field Safety Reminder
You don't need to be perfect, you need to act.

2-Sentence Safety Message
Early action makes the difference in a medical emergency. Recognize it, act
quickly, and stay with it until help arrives.

5 Quick Bullet Points
- Recognize the emergency early
- Call 911 and get an AED
- Start compressions if needed
- Control bleeding immediately
- Don't hesitate to act

Annex: First Aid Scope and Limits

Context
In a workplace emergency, our role is to provide immediate first aid and
CPR until higher-level medical care arrives. That role is limited and
defined.

What We Do (First Aid Scope)
- Recognize a medical emergency
- Call 911 or direct someone to call
- Use an AED if available
- Provide CPR if needed
- Control bleeding with direct pressure
- Keep the person stable and monitored
- Stay with the patient until EMS arrives

This is immediate care meant to preserve life, not provide full medical
treatment.

Where the Line Is
First aid is:
- Basic
- Immediate
- Non-invasive
- Focused on stabilization

Emergency Medical Services (EMT/Paramedic) care includes:
- Advanced assessment and diagnosis
- Airway management beyond basic positioning
- Medication administration
- Invasive procedures
- Transport decisions and ongoing medical care

Those actions are outside the scope of workplace first aid.

Why This Matters
Even if someone has prior medical training, when acting in a workplace
setting, the expectation is to operate within first aid scope unless
specifically authorized.

Staying within scope:
- Protects the patient
- Protects the responder
- Ensures a clear transition to EMS

Operational Takeaway
Act quickly, but stay within first aid and CPR. Stabilize the patient and
hand off to EMS.

Field Reminder
Your job is to keep them alive, not to treat everything.

Key Principle
Early action within scope saves lives. Overstepping scope creates risk.
