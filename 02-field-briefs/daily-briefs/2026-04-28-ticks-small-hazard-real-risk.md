Morning Safety Brief
Topic: Ticks – Small Hazard, Real Risk

Situation
Crews working in grass, brush, wooded areas, and around vegetation may be
exposed to ticks. They are small, often unnoticed, and attach to skin or
clothing during normal work activities.

Why It Matters
Ticks in Northern California can carry diseases, including Lyme disease and
other infections. Most bites are not felt, which means exposure often goes
unnoticed.
The longer a tick stays attached, the greater the risk of disease
transmission. Early detection and removal significantly reduce that risk.

Tick-Borne Disease (What to Know)
- Lyme disease is present in Northern California
- Risk increases the longer a tick is attached
- Early symptoms may include:
  - rash (often circular or expanding)
  - fever
  - fatigue
  - muscle or joint pain
- Symptoms may not appear immediately

Operational Hazards
- Ticks attaching without being noticed
- Delayed detection after the workday
- Improper removal increasing exposure risk
- Ignoring bites or early symptoms

Control Measures
- Wear long sleeves and long pants when working in vegetation
- Tuck pants into boots or socks when practical
- Use insect repellent when appropriate
- Avoid sitting or placing gear directly on the ground
- Perform tick checks during and after work

Where to Check
- Behind knees
- Waistline
- Armpits
- Neck and hairline
- Inside clothing and gear

Immediate First Aid (Tick Removal)
- Use tweezers to grasp the tick as close to the skin as possible
- Pull straight out with steady, even pressure
- Do not twist or crush the tick
- Clean the area with soap and water
- Wash hands after removal

What NOT to Do
- Do not burn the tick
- Do not use petroleum jelly or chemicals
- Do not squeeze the tick's body
- Do not ignore the bite after removal

Follow-Up
- Monitor for rash, fever, or flu-like symptoms
- Report exposure per company guidance
- Seek medical evaluation if symptoms develop

Stop Work and Control
If tick exposure is widespread and cannot be controlled, reassess the work
area and protective measures.

Field Safety Reminder
You won't feel a tick bite when it happens. The risk comes from how long it
goes unnoticed.

2-Sentence Safety Message
Ticks carry disease, but early removal greatly reduces risk. Check often,
remove quickly, and don't ignore symptoms.

5 Quick Bullet Points
- Check yourself and your gear often
- Remove ticks immediately
- Watch for symptoms after exposure
- Don't ignore a bite
- Control exposure before it becomes a problem

Annex: Lyme Disease – Awareness and Early Action

Context
Ticks in Northern California can carry Lyme disease. Not every tick bite
results in illness, but risk increases when a tick remains attached for an
extended period. Early recognition and action reduce that risk.

What Increases Risk
- Tick attached for an extended period (often >24–36 hours)
- Tick appears engorged (swollen with blood)
- Exposure in grassy, brushy, or wooded areas
- Delayed detection after the workday

What Lowers Risk
- Early detection and removal
- Proper removal technique
- Regular checks during and after work

Common Early Signs and Symptoms
- Expanding rash at the bite site (may resemble a "bullseye")
- Fever or chills
- Fatigue
- Headache
- Muscle or joint aches

Note: Symptoms may not appear immediately.

Immediate Actions (After Tick Removal)
- Clean the bite area with soap and water
- Note the date and location of the bite
- Monitor for symptoms over the following days and weeks

When to Seek Medical Evaluation
- Tick was attached for an extended period or appears engorged
- You are unsure how long it was attached
- Any symptoms develop (rash, fever, fatigue, body aches)
- Exposure occurred in a high-risk environment

Medical Considerations (Awareness Only)
A healthcare provider may evaluate risk and, in certain cases, consider
preventive treatment. This decision is time-sensitive and typically occurs
within a short window after tick removal.

What NOT to Do
- Do not ignore symptoms after a tick bite
- Do not delay evaluation if something feels off
- Do not assume a bite is harmless because it was not felt

Operational Takeaway
Most tick bites do not lead to illness if detected and managed early.
Risk increases with time, so early removal and follow-up awareness are
critical.

Field Safety Reminder
The risk isn't just the bite, it's how long it goes unnoticed.

Key Principle
Early recognition, early removal, and early follow-up reduce risk.
