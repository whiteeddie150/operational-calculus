Team,

Morning Safety Brief
Topic: The Ignorance of Success

Situation

One of the most dangerous lessons we can learn is the wrong lesson from a
successful outcome.

Most people assume failure is the greatest teacher.

In reality, failure usually provides feedback.

Success often does not.

A task is completed.
A hazard is missed.
A shortcut is taken.
A control is skipped.
Nothing bad happens.

The operation succeeds.

The danger is that the successful outcome can begin teaching the wrong
lesson.

Why It Matters

Human factors researchers refer to this as outcome bias.

We naturally judge the quality of a decision by the result rather than the
quality of the process that produced it. A poor decision that happens to
succeed can be viewed as a good decision simply because nothing went wrong.

Over time this can create a dangerous cycle:

- Deviation
- Success
- Increased confidence
- Reduced concern
- Repeated behavior
- Eventual consequence

The successful outcome becomes evidence that the shortcut was acceptable.

Operational Reality

Every experienced operator has seen this.

A crew:

- skips a step
- rushes a verification
- assumes conditions have not changed
- relies on memory instead of observation

Nothing happens.

The operation succeeds.

The lesson learned may become:

"That worked."

The lesson that should have been learned may be:

"We got away with it."

Those are not the same thing.

Connections to Previous Briefs

We have talked about:

- autopilot
- drift
- weak signals
- verification vs assumption
- tempo vs awareness
- information lag

The Ignorance of Success can feed all of them.

Success can quietly reduce:

- curiosity
- questioning
- reassessment
- verification

When that happens, assumptions begin replacing observation.

Where It Shows Up

In the Field

- Taking a shortcut because it worked before
- Assuming a control is still in place
- Skipping a second look
- Treating familiar work as predictable work

In the Office

- Assuming information is still current
- Relying on yesterday's operational picture
- Accepting information without clarification
- Believing previous success guarantees future success

Behavioral Warning Signs

- "We've always done it this way."
- "Nothing happened last time."
- "It worked before."
- Reduced questioning
- Reduced verification
- Increased confidence without reassessment

Operational Strength

Strong operators do not judge a decision solely by its outcome.

They ask:

- Was the process sound?
- Were assumptions verified?
- Were controls maintained?
- Would we make the same decision if conditions changed tomorrow?

Strong crews learn from both success and failure.

Connection to PG&E Standards and Doctrine

This supports:

- Questioning Attitude
- Verification Over Assumption
- Operational Learning
- SIF Prevention
- Human and Organizational Performance (HOP)

A questioning attitude is most important when everything appears to be
working.

That is when assumptions are least likely to be challenged.

Field Safety Reminder

A good outcome does not automatically validate the process that produced it.

2-Sentence Safety Message

Success can sometimes hide weaknesses that failure would immediately
expose. Strong operators continue verifying, questioning, and reassessing
even when previous outcomes have been positive.

5 Quick Bullet Points

- Success is not proof that the process was sound
- Verify even when things are going well
- Question assumptions reinforced by success
- Learn from good outcomes and bad outcomes
- Don't let success replace curiosity

Company Officer's Reminder

The most dangerous lesson is often the one learned from a successful
shortcut.
