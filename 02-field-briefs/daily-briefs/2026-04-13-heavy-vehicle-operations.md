Morning Safety Brief – Heavy Vehicle Operations (F-350 Super Duty Loads)

Topic: Safe Operation of Heavily Loaded Pickup Trucks (13,500–14,000 lbs)

Situation
Crews are operating Ford F-350 Super Duty units at or near maximum load
capacity. At these weights, vehicle performance changes significantly,
especially braking, handling, and stability. This vehicle should be treated
as a light commercial platform, not a standard pickup.

Why It Matters
Heavily loaded trucks require more time and distance to stop and are less
forgiving during sudden maneuvers. Loss of control incidents often occur
during braking, turning, or load shift events.

Federal data confirms:
- Loaded vehicles require substantially more stopping distance than
passenger vehicles
- Stopping distance increases as vehicle weight increases
- Real-world stopping distance can exceed 500 feet at highway speeds

Operational Hazards
- Increased stopping distance at highway speeds
- Brake fade during extended downhill or stop-and-go driving
- High center of gravity increasing rollover risk
- Tire overheating or blowout under heavy load
- Load shift affecting steering and stability
- Reduced acceleration impacting merging and crossing traffic

Control Measures
- Maintain following distance, minimum 6–8 seconds
- Begin braking early, avoid hard stops
- Use tow/haul mode and engine braking on grades
- Conduct pre-trip tire checks, verify pressure and load rating
- Confirm load is evenly distributed and secured
- Reduce speed on curves and ramps
- Avoid sudden steering inputs or aggressive lane changes

Key Data Point
According to FMCSA, large trucks can require up to 500 feet to stop at
highway speeds under real-world conditions.

Morning Safety Reminder
Drive it like it's a loaded system, not a pickup.
Give yourself time, give yourself space, and make every movement deliberate.
