MORNING SAFETY BRIEF

Topic: Listen to the First Complaint

Supporting PG&E Virtue: Curious

Situation

Many workplace injuries do not begin with a dramatic event.

They begin with something much smaller:

• A sore shoulder
• A tight lower back
• An awkward reach
• A weak grip
• A stiff knee
• An unusual amount of fatigue

These are often dismissed as "part of the job."

Sometimes they are.

Sometimes they are the first signal that something is changing.

Why It Matters

Most musculoskeletal injuries develop over time rather than appearing all
at once.

The body often provides information before an injury occurs.

The challenge is recognizing those signals while influence still remains.

Just as experienced operators watch for changes in weather, equipment
performance, or terrain conditions, we should also pay attention to changes
in our own physical condition.

The Science

NIOSH ergonomics research has shown that many workplace injuries are
associated with:

• Repetitive motion
• Awkward posture
• Forceful exertion
• Fatigue
• Insufficient recovery

The reportable injury often occurs near the end of a process that began
much earlier.

In many cases, the body was already providing information.

Human Performance Perspective

In previous briefs we discussed weak signals.

A weak signal is not a problem.

A weak signal is information about a potential future problem.

The same principle applies to the body.

Increasing soreness.

Increasing stiffness.

Increasing fatigue.

Increasing compensation.

These may not be injuries.

But they may be signals that the trajectory is changing.

Operational Awareness

Ask yourself:

• Am I working differently than I was last week?
• Is a task requiring more effort than it normally does?
• Am I compensating for discomfort?
• Have I adjusted my posture or movement because something hurts?
• Am I ignoring information because the task still gets completed?

The body is often the first monitoring system.

Pay attention to what it is telling you.

Questioning Attitude

PG&E's Questioning Attitude encourages us to prefer facts over assumptions.

Don't assume:

"It's just soreness."

Instead ask:

• What changed?
• Why is this occurring?
• Is it improving or getting worse?
• What happens if I continue this way for another week?

Supporting PG&E Virtue

Curious

Curiosity applies to more than equipment and job sites.

Curiosity also means investigating changes in our own performance and
condition before they become larger problems.

Field Application

Today:

• Adjust your work position before discomfort becomes pain
• Use equipment and harnesses correctly
• Alternate tasks when possible
• Pay attention to awkward reaches and repetitive movements
• Address small issues while they are still small

Safety Reminder

The best time to address an ergonomic injury is before it becomes an injury.

The earlier a problem is recognized, the more options remain available.

2-Sentence Safety Message

Most injuries begin as small changes that are easy to dismiss. Recognizing
those changes early allows us to take action while influence still remains
and before the condition becomes harder to correct.

5 Quick Bullet Points

• Pay attention to increasing soreness or stiffness
• Treat discomfort as information, not an inconvenience
• Watch for changes in posture or movement
• Correct awkward positions early
• Address small issues before they become large ones

Closing Thought

We often talk about weak signals in equipment, weather, and operations.
Remember that your body produces weak signals too. The safest operators are
often the ones who recognize those signals early and make adjustments
before the outcome is decided.

NIOSH Musculoskeletal Health Research

NIOSH research consistently shows that musculoskeletal disorders are often
the result of cumulative exposure rather than a single event. Repetitive
motion, awkward posture, forceful exertion, and fatigue can gradually
increase injury risk over time.

Practical Takeaway:
Many injuries begin long before the day they are reported.

---

OSHA Ergonomics Principles

OSHA identifies awkward posture, repetitive motion, force, and duration as
major contributors to workplace musculoskeletal injuries.

Practical Takeaway:
The body's position often matters as much as the task itself.

---

James Reason – Human Error

Reason's work suggests major failures are often preceded by smaller
precursor conditions that go unrecognized or unaddressed.

Practical Takeaway:
The injury is often the final event in a chain that started much earlier.

---

Sidney Dekker – Drift Into Failure

Dekker observed that people frequently adapt to discomfort, inefficiencies,
and workarounds because they continue to achieve successful outcomes.

Practical Takeaway:
Just because the work gets done does not mean the process is healthy or
sustainable.

---

Human Performance Connection

Weak signals are early indicators that conditions may be changing.

The body produces weak signals just as equipment does:

• Tightness
• Soreness
• Fatigue
• Reduced mobility
• Compensation

Practical Takeaway:
Pain is not always damage, but it is always information worth understanding.
