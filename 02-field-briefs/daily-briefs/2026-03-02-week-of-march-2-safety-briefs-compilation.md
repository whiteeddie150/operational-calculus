Date: Tuesday, March 3, 2026
Topic: Situational Awareness and Changing Conditions

Situation

By Tuesday, work is fully underway and job sites begin to evolve. Traffic
patterns shift, equipment moves, weather and ground conditions change, and
new hazards can develop that were not present during initial planning.

Why It Matters

Many incidents occur when workers rely on initial assessments and fail to
recognize changing conditions. Maintaining situational awareness throughout
the job prevents injuries caused by moving hazards, environmental changes,
and unexpected exposures.

Operational Hazards

• New obstacles or debris in work areas
• Changing traffic patterns or pedestrian activity
• Equipment movement and shifting work zones
• Ground instability, mud, or uneven surfaces
• Distractions leading to "autopilot" behavior

Control Measures

• Reassess hazards throughout the shift, not just at startup
• Pause and scan the work area before each new task
• Communicate changes in conditions to the crew immediately
• Maintain clear escape routes and safe positioning
• Stay present and avoid rushing or working on autopilot

Relevant Statistics from Federal Data

- OSHA identifies hazard recognition and ongoing assessment as critical
components of injury prevention.
- The National Safety Council reports that many workplace incidents occur
when workers fail to detect changing conditions.

Date: Wednesday, March 4, 2026
Topic: Communication and Team Coordination

Situation

By midweek, multiple tasks, crews, and equipment may be operating in the
same area. Without clear communication, assumptions can replace
coordination, increasing the risk of injuries, equipment damage, and public
exposure.

Why It Matters

Poor communication is a contributing factor in many workplace incidents.
When messages are unclear, incomplete, or not acknowledged, crews may act
on different assumptions, leading to unsafe positioning, unexpected
equipment movement, or missed hazards.

Operational Hazards

• Assumptions about who is responsible for a task
• Equipment movement without clear signals or confirmation
• Missed radio calls or unclear instructions
• Overlapping work zones without coordination
• Hesitation to speak up about concerns

Control Measures

• Use clear, direct communication and confirm understanding
• Establish hand signals, radio channels, or communication methods before
work
• Announce equipment movement and verify all-clear before operating
• Encourage crew members to speak up if instructions are unclear
• Pause work if communication breaks down until clarity is restored

Relevant Statistics from Federal Data

- OSHA identifies communication failures as a contributing factor in many
workplace incidents.
- The National Safety Council emphasizes clear communication as a key
element of effective safety programs.

Date: Thursday, March 5, 2026
Topic: Human Factors – Distraction and Cognitive Overload

Situation

As the week progresses, crews juggle multiple tasks, radio traffic, public
interactions, and time pressures. Mental workload can increase, leading to
distraction and cognitive overload that reduce focus on critical safety
steps.

Why It Matters

Distraction and divided attention are major contributors to workplace
incidents. When mental bandwidth is exceeded, workers may miss hazards,
skip steps, or make errors in judgment that place themselves, coworkers,
and the public at risk.

Operational Hazards

• Divided attention from radios, phones, and multiple conversations
• Task switching without completing critical safety steps
• Rushing due to schedule pressure or competing priorities
• Missing hazards while focused on a single task
• Reduced situational awareness from cognitive overload

Control Measures

• Pause and refocus before starting safety-critical steps
• Complete one task before shifting to another when possible
• Limit unnecessary distractions during high-risk activities
• Use clear communication to reduce confusion and repeated instructions
• Speak up if you feel overloaded or unsure about the next step

Relevant Statistics from Federal Data

- The National Safety Council identifies distraction and cognitive
overload as contributors to workplace errors and injuries.
- OSHA emphasizes that human factors, including mental workload and
attention, affect hazard recognition and decision-making.

Date: Friday, March 6, 2026
Topic: Weekend Mindset and Personal Risk Transition

Situation

As Friday progresses, attention naturally shifts toward weekend plans,
travel, home projects, and family responsibilities. This mental transition
can reduce focus during the final tasks of the workweek.

Why It Matters

Many incidents occur near the end of shifts when attention drifts and
workers begin thinking about what comes next. Losing focus during final
steps can lead to injuries, equipment damage, or leaving hazards for
others. PG&E's safety culture emphasizes that safety must remain the top
priority and that employees are accountable for protecting coworkers and
the public.

Operational Hazards

• Rushing to finish tasks before the weekend
• Skipping final checks or cleanup
• Reduced attention during equipment shutdown or securement
• Leaving tools, materials, or hazards for the next crew
• Mental distraction from weekend plans

Control Measures

• Stay focused through the final task and last step
• Complete shutdown, lockout, and securement procedures fully
• Conduct a final site check before leaving
• Leave the work area safe for the next crew and the public
• Transition to weekend mindset only after the job is complete

Relevant Federal Safety Guidance

Effective safety programs rely on hazard recognition, communication, and
accountability throughout all phases of work, including task completion.
Safety organizations also note increased incident risk during transitions
and end-of-shift periods due to reduced attention.

Supporting Language from PG&E Governing Documents

PG&E states that "nothing is more important to us than safety" and
emphasizes that employees must "speak up, listen up and follow up" to
address hazards and protect the public. Leaving a worksite safe and secure
reflects this commitment to accountability and community protection.

Supporting Language from IBEW 1245 Physical Agreement

The Company and Union affirm that "nothing is more important than the
safety of PG&E employees, contractors, and the public," and commit to joint
cooperation to sustain a strong safety culture. Completing work safely and
leaving the site hazard-free supports this shared responsibility.

Field Safety Reminder

Finish the job, then shift your focus. Leave the site safe, secure the
equipment, and head into the weekend knowing you did it right.

2-Sentence Safety Message

Friday brings weekend plans and distractions, but the job is not done until
the site is safe and secure. Stay focused through the final step and leave
the work area safe for the next crew and the public.

5 Quick Bullet Points

• Do not rush the final steps of the job
• Complete shutdown and securement procedures
• Conduct a final site check before leaving
• Leave the area safe for others and the public
• Shift to weekend mindset after the job is complete
