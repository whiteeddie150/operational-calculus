MORNING SAFETY BRIEF

Topic: Recovery Is Part of the Work

Supporting PG&E Virtue: Owners

Situation

Many of today's tasks require sustained physical effort, repetitive
movement, equipment operation, driving, walking uneven terrain, and
exposure to heat and environmental conditions.

Most safety discussions focus on the work itself.

Today, let's focus on recovery.

Because recovery is not what happens after the work.

Recovery is part of the work.

Why It Matters

Every task we perform draws from our physical and mental capacity.

We often monitor:

• Fuel levels
• Water supply
• Equipment condition
• Battery life
• Weather conditions

Yet one of the most important resources we manage is our own capacity.

The body does not become stronger during work.

The body adapts and restores itself during recovery.

The Science

NIOSH and occupational health research consistently show that cumulative
fatigue and musculoskeletal injuries are influenced by both workload and
recovery.

Exposure accumulates.

Fatigue accumulates.

Stress accumulates.

Recovery helps restore the capacity needed to safely perform tomorrow's
work.

Without adequate recovery, today's workload becomes tomorrow's limitation.

Human Performance Perspective

Recovery is not simply about sleep.

Recovery includes:

• Hydration
• Nutrition
• Physical recovery
• Mental recovery
• Managing accumulated fatigue

Two workers may perform the same job today.

The worker who recovers more effectively often returns with greater
capacity, better awareness, and reduced injury risk.

Operational Awareness

Today's Questioning Attitude challenge is:

How much capacity am I bringing to today's work?

Ask yourself:

• Am I carrying fatigue from previous days?
• Am I recovering as intentionally as I am working?
• Is soreness improving or accumulating?
• Am I restoring capacity or simply getting through another day?

The body often provides signals long before an injury occurs.

Pay attention to them.

PG&E Doctrine Alignment

PG&E's Questioning Attitude encourages a preference for facts over
assumptions.

Don't assume your body is fully recovered.

Assess it honestly.

Recognize where fatigue, soreness, or reduced performance may be
influencing your work.

Supporting PG&E Virtue

Owners

Ownership includes taking responsibility for the condition of the equipment
we use, the work we perform, and the capability we bring to the job.

Our bodies are one of the most important tools we have.

Maintaining that capability is part of ownership.

Field Application

Today:

• Hydrate throughout the shift
• Take recovery opportunities when available
• Pay attention to accumulated soreness and fatigue
• Use proper technique even when tired
• Recognize that recovery begins before the workday ends

Safety Reminder

The body keeps score.

Every day of work adds to either accumulated fatigue or long-term
resilience.

Recovery helps determine which path we are on.

2-Sentence Safety Message

Work creates the demand. Recovery restores the capacity. Both are necessary
for sustained performance, long-term health, and safe operations.

5 Quick Bullet Points

• Monitor your capacity, not just the task
• Hydrate before you feel behind
• Pay attention to accumulated fatigue
• Use recovery opportunities throughout the day
• Treat recovery as part of the job, not something separate from it
