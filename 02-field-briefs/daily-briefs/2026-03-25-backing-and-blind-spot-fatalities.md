Tailboard Safety Briefing

Topic: Backing and Blind Spot Fatalities

---

Situation

Most serious vehicle incidents don't happen at speed. They happen when a
truck backs up, turns, or repositions, and someone is standing where the
operator can't see them.

---

Why It Matters

This is how people get hit.
Low speed, routine movement, no eye contact, and someone assumes they're
visible when they're not. OSHA identifies struck-by incidents as a leading
cause of worker fatalities, particularly involving vehicles and equipment.

---

Operational Hazards

• Standing behind or beside equipment without eye contact
• Walking into a backing path
• Assuming the operator sees you
• Multiple personnel moving around equipment
• No clear spotter or breakdown in communication

---

Control Measures

• Make eye contact before entering any work zone
• If you lose sight of the operator, stop moving
• Use a spotter when backing if visibility is limited
• Stay out of blind spots unless acknowledged
• Stop equipment movement immediately if communication is lost

---

Relevant Federal Safety Guidance

Struck-by incidents are one of OSHA's "Fatal Four" leading causes of
workplace deaths, with vehicle and equipment movement being a primary
factor.
NIOSH identifies blind spots and backing operations as key contributors to
worker fatalities in mobile equipment environments.

---

PG&E states that "nothing is more important to us than safety" and
reinforces that employees must speak up, listen up, and follow up to
protect coworkers and the public.
Maintaining clear communication and accountability around equipment
movement directly supports this expectation.

---

Supporting Language from IBEW 1245 Physical Agreement

The Company and Union affirm that the safety of employees, contractors,
and the public is the highest priority and commit to maintaining safe

---

Field Safety Reminder

If they can't see you, you're already in danger. Don't rely on assumptions,
rely on eye contact.

---

2-Sentence Safety Message

Most workers who get struck by equipment thought they were visible. Make
eye contact, stay out of blind spots, and never assume the operator sees
you.

---

5 Quick Bullet Points

• No eye contact = no movement
• Stay out of blind spots
• Use a spotter when backing
• Stop if communication is lost
• Never assume you're seen
