Tailboard Safety Briefing

Date: February 26, 2026
Topic: Line of Fire Awareness

---

Situation

During daily tasks, crews work around moving equipment, suspended loads,
energized components, pressurized systems, and shifting materials. These
conditions create line of fire exposures where a person can be struck,
caught, or crushed.

---

Why It Matters

Line of fire incidents often result in serious injury or fatality because
they involve high force, stored energy, or moving equipment. Many occur
during routine positioning or when workers place themselves in harm's way
without recognizing the exposure.

---

Operational Hazards

• Standing under suspended or shifting loads
• Positioning between equipment and fixed objects
• Stored energy release from tensioned lines, springs, or pressurized
systems
• Equipment swing radius and blind spots
• Materials rolling, sliding, or shifting unexpectedly

---

Control Measures

• Identify line of fire hazards before starting work
• Stay clear of suspended loads and never position beneath them
• Maintain safe distance from moving equipment and swing radiuses
• Control stored energy before working on tensioned or pressurized systems
• Communicate clearly during lifts and equipment movement
• Reposition yourself if you feel exposed to potential movement

---

Relevant Statistics from Federal Data

- OSHA identifies struck-by and caught-between incidents as leading causes
of serious workplace injuries and fatalities in high-risk industries.
- The Bureau of Labor Statistics consistently reports struck-by incidents
among the top causes of occupational fatalities.

---

2-Minute Narrative

Today's focus is line of fire awareness. Many serious injuries happen when
someone is struck by moving equipment, caught between objects, or exposed
to stored energy. These incidents often occur during routine positioning
when we place ourselves in harm's way without recognizing the risk.

Suspended loads, swing radiuses, tensioned lines, and shifting materials
all create line of fire hazards. A load can shift, equipment can move, or
stored energy can release without warning. If you are in that path, you are
relying on luck.

The controls are simple. Identify line of fire hazards before starting.
Stay clear of suspended loads. Maintain distance from moving equipment.
Control stored energy before working on systems. Communicate during lifts
and reposition if you feel exposed.

If you are in the line of fire, move. Do not wait for the hazard to move
you.
