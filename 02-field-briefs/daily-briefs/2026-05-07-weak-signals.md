Weak Signals – The Job Usually Tells Us Before It Goes Bad

Core Concept:
Most serious problems are preceded by small indicators that something is
wrong. High-performing crews recognize and act on those indicators early
instead of waiting for obvious failure.

- different sound/vibration from equipment

Annex: Weak Signals – Research and Operational Foundations

Igor Ansoff – Weak Signals Theory
Work: Managing Strategic Surprise by Response to Weak Signals (1975)

Contribution:
Ansoff introduced the idea that major failures and disruptive events are
often preceded by small, incomplete indicators that are easy to dismiss
early on. He showed that organizations frequently receive warning signs
long before a problem becomes obvious.

Key Takeaway:
Big problems usually begin as small signals that were not recognized or
acted upon early enough.

---

Karl Weick & Kathleen Sutcliffe – High Reliability Organizations
Work: Managing the Unexpected

Contribution:
Weick and Sutcliffe showed that high-performing organizations stay
sensitive to subtle operational changes and avoid oversimplifying
anomalies. Their work emphasized continuous awareness and questioning
assumptions in high-risk environments.

Key Takeaway:
Strong organizations pay attention to small inconsistencies before they
become major failures.

---

Aviation Human Factors Research
Sources: FAA Human Factors Guides and Aviation Safety Studies

Contribution:
Aviation research consistently found that accidents are often preceded by
weak signals such as communication confusion, procedural deviations,
unusual instrument indications, or subtle changes in conditions that were
noticed but discounted.

Key Takeaway:
Small anomalies are often early warnings, not random noise.

---

Nuclear and Process Safety Research
Sources: High Reliability and Process Safety Studies

Contribution:
Research in nuclear power and industrial process safety found that
catastrophic failures rarely occur without warning. Systems typically show
precursor events, abnormal conditions, or degraded controls before major
incidents occur.

Key Takeaway:
Systems usually "whisper" before they fail.

---

Sidney Dekker – Human Error and Drift
Work: Drift into Failure

Contribution:
Dekker showed that systems gradually move away from safe boundaries through
normal operational decisions and adaptations. Weak signals are often
present during this drift but become normalized or ignored over time.

Key Takeaway:
Failure develops gradually and often announces itself early.

---

James Reason – Human Error
Work: Human Error

Contribution:
Reason demonstrated that accidents occur when multiple small failures and
gaps align. Weak signals often represent early holes forming in the system
before a larger breakdown occurs.

Key Takeaway:
Major failures are usually built from multiple small problems that were not
corrected.

---

Operational Connection

Across all of this research, the conclusion is consistent:
- systems rarely fail suddenly
- weak signals appear early
- assumptions suppress recognition
- drift occurs when signals go unchallenged
- strong organizations recognize and act before failure becomes obvious

Field Application

Weak signals may include:
- conditions not matching the plan
- unusual sounds, behavior, or communication
- repeated small errors
- confusion or uncertainty
- work slowly moving outside expected conditions

Key Principle

Most failures announce themselves early. The challenge is recognizing the
signal before it becomes obvious.
