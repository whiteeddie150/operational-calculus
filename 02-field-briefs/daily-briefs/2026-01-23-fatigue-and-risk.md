Here is a short safety brief you can deliver in about 2 minutes. It
includes credible statistics and clear behaviors people can act on
immediately. Topic stays on fatigue and decision-making, since it resonates
across most work environments.

Safety Brief: Fatigue and Risk

Fatigue is not just about feeling tired. It directly degrades judgment.

According to the National Safety Council, fatigue is a contributing factor
in roughly 13 percent of workplace injuries. The Centers for Disease
Control and Prevention reports that being awake for 17 hours straight
produces impairment similar to a blood alcohol level of 0.05 percent, and
at 24 hours, impairment is comparable to 0.10 percent, above the legal
driving limit in every state.

In other words, fatigue changes how we assess risk long before we feel
exhausted.

What fatigue looks like on the job:

Rushing routine tasks
Skipping checks or steps you normally follow
Overconfidence in familiar work
Narrow focus, missing changes in the environment

Three behaviors that reduce fatigue-related risk today:

Slow the start
Take 30 seconds before beginning a task to mentally walk through the first
three steps. This forces your brain back into active mode.

Verbalize uncertainty
If something feels "off," say it out loud. Fatigue thrives in silence.
Speaking up interrupts poor decision momentum.

Use micro-resets
Brief resets work. Hydrate, stretch, or pause for one minute between
tasks. Evidence shows short breaks improve attention and reduce error
rates.

Fatigue follows a known pattern, and recognizing that pattern is what makes
it manageable. That is the same principle behind Gordon Graham's
public-safety risk-management work: recognizable, recurring hazards can be
controlled before they cause harm. The control here is awareness and small
behavioral checks.

Watch yourself. Watch each other. Do not let fatigue make decisions for
you.
