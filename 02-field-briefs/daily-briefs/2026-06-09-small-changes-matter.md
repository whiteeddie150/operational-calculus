MORNING SAFETY BRIEF

Topic: Small Changes Matter

Supporting PG&E Virtue: Curious

Situation

As fire season continues to build across California, many of us will be
working in environments where conditions can change rapidly. One of the
lessons repeatedly identified in safety science, firefighter fatality
investigations, and high-reliability organizations is that major incidents
rarely begin as major incidents.

More often, they begin as small changes that were noticed but not fully
understood.

Why It Matters

The National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health (NIOSH) has
investigated hundreds of firefighter fatalities and serious injuries. While
each incident is unique, many investigations reveal a common pattern:
communication challenges, changing conditions, degraded situational
awareness, and evolving hazards often existed before the critical event
occurred.

The lesson is not that every small change predicts a serious outcome.

The lesson is that significant events are often preceded by indicators that
conditions are changing.

Safety science refers to these indicators as weak signals.

Examples include:

• Conditions that do not match expectations
• Access routes becoming more difficult
• Changing weather or smoke behavior
• Communication becoming less effective
• Equipment behaving differently than normal
• Work progressing differently than expected

Operational Awareness

Experienced operators understand that major problems rarely arrive without
warning.

The challenge is recognizing when small changes represent normal variation
and when they signal a meaningful change in conditions.

Questions that support operational awareness include:

• What has changed?
• What looks different today?
• Does this match my expectations?
• What am I assuming?
• Who else needs this information?

PG&E Doctrine Alignment

PG&E's SIF Prevention guidance emphasizes the importance of maintaining a
Questioning Attitude.

"A questioning attitude promotes a preference for facts over assumptions
and opinions."

This principle exists because conditions change.

The fact that something was true yesterday does not guarantee it remains
true today.

A questioning attitude helps us verify conditions, challenge assumptions,
and identify changes before they become problems.

Supporting PG&E Virtue

Curious

Operationally, curiosity is not simply learning something new.

Curiosity means remaining engaged enough to notice when reality no longer
matches expectations.

Curious operators investigate small changes rather than dismissing them.

Human Performance Perspective

Research in human performance and decision-making suggests that experienced
operators often recognize changes before they can fully explain them.

The goal is not to react to every anomaly.

The goal is to remain attentive to information that suggests conditions may
be evolving.

Small changes deserve attention because they often represent the earliest
opportunity to learn, adapt, and make informed decisions.

Field Application

As operational tempo increases during fire season:

• Verify changing conditions.
• Communicate emerging concerns early.
• Challenge assumptions created by familiarity.
• Pay attention to information that does not fit expectations.
• Help maintain a common operating picture.

Safety Reminder

Major incidents rarely arrive all at once.

More often, they begin as small changes that were noticed but not fully
understood.

2-Sentence Safety Message

Small changes are often the earliest indication that conditions are
evolving. Maintaining a questioning attitude and remaining curious about
changing conditions helps keep awareness aligned with reality.

5 Quick Bullet Points

• Notice what has changed
• Prefer facts over assumptions
• Communicate emerging concerns early
• Verify conditions before acting
• Stay curious when work becomes familiar
