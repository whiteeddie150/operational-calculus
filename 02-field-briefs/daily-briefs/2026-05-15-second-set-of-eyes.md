Team,

Friday Safety Brief
Topic: Second Set of Eyes – Strong Crews Catch What Individuals Miss

Situation
Even experienced operators miss things.

Fatigue, repetition, distraction, familiarity, and changing conditions can
all slowly degrade awareness over the course of a week. That is normal
human performance, not weakness.

Strong operations are built around crews helping catch what individuals may
not see in the moment.

Why It Matters
Research in aviation, human factors, and high-reliability operations
consistently shows that one of the strongest safety controls in any
operation is another engaged and experienced set of eyes.

High-performing crews:
- cross-check each other
- verify assumptions
- question unclear conditions
- help catch drift before it compounds

Operational Risk
When crews stop actively checking each other:
- assumptions spread
- weak signals get missed
- verification degrades
- small issues become normalized

Most operational failures are not caused by one major mistake.

They are usually multiple small misses that nobody interrupted early enough.

Connections to Previous Briefs
We've talked before about:
- drift
- weak signals
- verification vs assumption
- autopilot
- interruption recovery
- cumulative fatigue

This is one of the ways strong crews help prevent those issues from
compounding.

Good crews:
- keep the room honest
- challenge assumptions respectfully
- help maintain awareness when workload, fatigue, or repetition start
degrading attention

Where It Shows Up

In the Field
- pre-job briefs
- switching and tagging
- equipment checks
- spotting changing conditions
- catching missed steps before exposure occurs

In the Office
- planning reviews
- email clarification
- questioning assumptions
- identifying gaps or missing information
- cross-checking decisions before execution

Behavioral Warning Signs
- "I already checked it"
- assumption-based agreement
- nobody questioning conditions
- silent uncertainty
- reduced curiosity
- going through the motions

Operational Strength
Strong crews do not rely on one person maintaining perfect awareness all
day.

They:
- cross-check
- communicate plainly
- verify critical details
- help catch drift early
- protect each other from assumption and fatigue

Connection to PG&E Standards
This directly supports:
- questioning attitude
- verification over assumption
- speak-up culture
- SIF prevention
- operational learning

Field Safety Reminder
Strong crews don't just trust each other. They actively verify and support
each other.

2-Sentence Safety Message
Human performance naturally degrades under fatigue, repetition, and
workload. One of the strongest safety controls in any operation is another
engaged and experienced set of eyes.

5 Quick Bullet Points
- Cross-check critical work
- Verify instead of assuming
- Speak up early about concerns
- Help catch drift before it compounds
- Protect the crew from missed details
