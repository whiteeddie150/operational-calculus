Morning Safety Brief
Topic: Risk Drift and Communication Gaps in Fire Conditions

Situation
We are operating in fire conditions where weather, fuels, and active work
are all interacting at the same time. In many cases, PG&E crews are not on
the same communication system as fire resources, which means our
situational awareness may not match current fire behavior.

Why It Matters
Serious incidents rarely come from one obvious failure. They develop when
conditions trend in the wrong direction and that change is not recognized
early.
The 1953 Rattlesnake Fire demonstrated how crews remained engaged because
no single factor triggered a stop, but conditions stacked until they
changed faster than crews could react.

Operational Hazards
- Changing fire behavior not recognized early
- Delayed or indirect communication from fire resources
- Crews operating with an incomplete picture of conditions
- Lack of defined stop-work triggers
- Continued work based on outdated information

Control Measures
- Identify who is monitoring fire conditions for your crew
- Confirm how updates will be received and how often
- Watch for trends, not just current conditions
- Define stop-work triggers before starting work
- Slow down and reassess if information is unclear or delayed

Field Safety Reminder
If you don't know how conditions are changing, you don't have full
situational awareness.

2-Sentence Safety Message
Conditions become dangerous when they trend worse, not just when they look
bad. If communication or situational awareness is unclear, slow down and
reassess.

5 Quick Bullet Points
- Conditions become dangerous when they trend worse, not just when they
look bad
- Expect delays if you are not on the same communication system as fire
resources
- If you don't know who is tracking the fire, you don't have full
awareness
- Define stop-work triggers before conditions force the decision
- Speak up and act early if something doesn't feel right
