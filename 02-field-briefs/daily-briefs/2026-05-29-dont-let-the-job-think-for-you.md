Team,

Morning Safety Brief
Topic: Don't Let the Job Think for You

Situation

Experience is one of our greatest assets. It can also quietly become a
liability when familiarity starts replacing deliberate thinking.

The more often we perform a task, the easier it becomes for the brain to
operate from memory instead of observation. That is not laziness. It is
normal human behavior.

The challenge is making sure experience supports awareness rather than
replacing it.

Why It Matters

Strong operators recognize patterns. Strong operators also understand that
patterns are not reality.

As work becomes familiar:
- assumptions become easier
- verification becomes less frequent
- curiosity begins fading
- subtle changes become harder to recognize

Most operational problems do not occur because people lack experience.

They occur when experience convinces us we already know what we are about
to see.

Operational Reality

Every operation contains changing conditions.

Equipment changes.
Environmental conditions change.
Priorities change.
Information changes.
People change.

The fact that something looked a certain way yesterday does not guarantee
it looks the same today.

Connections to Previous Briefs

We have talked about:
- drift
- weak signals
- autopilot
- tempo vs awareness
- information lag
- communication compression
- crew cross-checking

This topic sits underneath all of them.

Familiarity can quietly lead to:
- autopilot
- assumption-based thinking
- reduced verification
- degraded situational awareness

Where It Shows Up

In the Field
- skipping a recheck
- assuming conditions have not changed
- relying on memory instead of observation
- treating familiar work as predictable work

In the Office
- operating from yesterday's information
- assuming an email says what you expected it to say
- accepting information without clarification
- relying on past experience instead of current conditions

Behavioral Warning Signs

- "I've done this a thousand times."
- "It's probably the same as last time."
- reduced questioning
- automatic decision-making
- diminished curiosity
- assumption replacing verification

Operational Strength

Strong operators stay deliberately engaged with the work.

They:
- verify what they think they know
- reassess changing conditions
- maintain curiosity
- challenge assumptions
- remain attentive even when work becomes routine

Experience is strongest when paired with verification.

Connection to PG&E Standards and Doctrine

This supports:
- questioning attitude
- verification over assumption
- operational awareness
- SIF prevention
- Human and Organizational Performance (HOP)

PG&E's questioning attitude exists because conditions change and
assumptions fail.

Field Safety Reminder

Familiar work still deserves deliberate attention.

2-Sentence Safety Message

Experience helps us recognize patterns, but patterns are not reality.
Strong operators continue verifying conditions even when the work feels
familiar.

5 Quick Bullet Points

- Familiar work still deserves attention
- Verify what you think you know
- Stay curious about changing conditions
- Experience is strongest when paired with verification
- Don't let familiarity do the thinking for you

Company Officer's Reminder

The most experienced person on the crew is not the one who assumes the
least risk. It is usually the one who continues checking, questioning, and
verifying long after the work has become familiar.

AUTOPILOT

Technical Definition:
A state in which familiar work is guided primarily by automatic cognitive
processes rather than active observation, deliberate verification, and
conscious decision-making.

Operational Definition:
The tendency for familiar work to be guided by expectation and habit rather
than active observation and verification.

Plain-Language Definition:
The job starts thinking for you.

Primary Hazard:
Expectation begins replacing observation.

Primary Countermeasure:
Verification and deliberate curiosity.
