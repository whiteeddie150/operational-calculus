MORNING SAFETY BRIEF

Topic: The Injury You Don't Notice

Supporting PG&E Virtue: Owners

Situation

Today's work around transmission structures will involve prolonged
weed-eater operations, uneven terrain, repetitive movements, sustained grip
force, vibration, and extended time on task.

Unlike many workplace hazards, repetitive motion injuries rarely occur from
a single event.

They develop gradually through thousands of small exposures accumulated
over days, months, and years.

Why It Matters

According to the Bureau of Labor Statistics, musculoskeletal disorders,
including repetitive motion and cumulative trauma injuries, consistently
rank among the leading causes of workplace injuries resulting in lost
workdays in the United States.

These injuries affect:

• Shoulders
• Elbows
• Wrists
• Hands
• Knees
• Lower back

The challenge is that the injury often develops long before it becomes
serious enough to stop work.

By the time many workers seek treatment, the exposure has been accumulating
for months or years.

The Science

OSHA and NIOSH identify several factors that increase the risk of
repetitive motion injuries:

• Repetition
• Forceful exertion
• Awkward posture
• Vibration
• Duration of exposure

Our work often contains all five.

The risk is not the first hour of operation.

The risk is the cumulative effect of performing the same movements
repeatedly while fatigue gradually changes how the work is performed.

Human Performance Perspective

Research shows that as muscles fatigue:

• Grip strength declines
• Posture changes
• Technique changes
• Force distribution changes

Most of these adjustments occur without conscious awareness.

The body naturally compensates.

The problem is that compensations intended to reduce fatigue can increase
stress on joints, tendons, and muscles.

Operational Awareness

Today's question is not:

"Has the work changed?"

The work is largely the same.

The better question is:

"Has my body changed?"

Ask yourself:

• Am I gripping the tool harder than I was this morning?
• Am I leaning more than I was earlier?
• Am I twisting differently?
• Am I fighting the equipment rather than guiding it?
• Has fatigue changed my technique?

Small changes in technique repeated hundreds of times can become large
exposures by the end of the day.

PG&E Doctrine Alignment

PG&E's SIF Prevention guidance promotes a Questioning Attitude and a
preference for facts over assumptions.

That principle applies to our own physical condition as well.

Don't assume your body is performing the same way at 1400 hours that it was
at 0800 hours.

Pay attention to the facts.

Pay attention to fatigue.

Supporting PG&E Virtue

Owners

Ownership includes taking responsibility for the condition of the tools we
use, the work we perform, and the body that allows us to perform it.

Recognizing fatigue early and making small adjustments today can help
prevent chronic injuries tomorrow.

Safety Reminder

Most repetitive motion injuries do not begin with a sudden event.

They begin with small changes that go unnoticed and uncorrected.

2-Sentence Safety Message

Repetitive motion injuries are among the most common workplace injuries in
America because they develop gradually through repeated exposure rather
than a single event. Pay attention to fatigue, posture, and technique
throughout the day because small adjustments repeated hundreds of times
matter.

5 Quick Bullet Points

• Monitor fatigue throughout the shift
• Maintain a relaxed grip whenever possible
• Change position and posture frequently
• Pay attention to how your body feels, not just the task
• Speak up early when discomfort begins developing
