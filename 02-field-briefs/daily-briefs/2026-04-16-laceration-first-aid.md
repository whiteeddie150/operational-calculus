Topic: Safety Doesn't End at Prevention – Laceration First Aid

Situation
Cuts and lacerations are one of the most common injuries in field work.
Even with proper controls, sharp edges, tools, and materials can still
result in injury.

Why It Matters
Safety doesn't end at prevention. When an injury occurs, the actions taken
in the first few minutes can significantly reduce severity and improve
outcomes.
Our role is not advanced medical care, it is immediate first aid to control
bleeding and stabilize the situation until higher-level care is available.

Operational Hazards
- Contact with sharp tools, metal edges, and equipment
- Slips or loss of control while cutting or handling materials
- Improper tool use or damaged equipment
- Working in tight or awkward positions
- Delayed recognition of injury or bleeding

Control Measures
- Inspect tools and materials before use
- Maintain proper hand placement and body positioning
- Use appropriate PPE (gloves, sleeves, eye protection)
- Keep work areas stable and controlled
- Stop work immediately if an injury occurs

Immediate First Aid (Lacerations)
- Ensure the scene is safe before assisting
- Apply direct pressure to the wound using a clean cloth or dressing
- Maintain steady pressure to control bleeding
- Elevate the injured area if practical and it does not cause more harm
- Use a bandage to secure the dressing once bleeding is controlled
- Seek medical evaluation for deep, large, or uncontrolled wounds

What NOT to Do
- Do not perform advanced procedures beyond basic first aid
- Do not attempt wound closure (stitches, adhesives, etc.)
- Do not remove deeply embedded objects
- Do not delay care if bleeding is not controlled
- Do not assume the injury is minor without proper evaluation

Addendum: Scope Differentiation – First Aid vs EMT-Level Care

Context
Many of our crews have EMT or paramedic backgrounds. That experience is
valuable, but the work environment does not support full EMS operations.
Our responsibility is to provide immediate first aid, not to operate as a
medical unit.

Scope Differences

Environment
- First Aid: Performed in uncontrolled field environments with limited
equipment and support
- EMT-Level Care: Performed within a structured EMS system with equipment,
protocols, and medical oversight

Authority
- First Aid: Based on basic training and workplace expectations
- EMT-Level Care: Operates under medical direction, protocols, and
licensure

Objective
- First Aid: Control the problem and stabilize the patient
- EMT-Level Care: Assess, treat, and manage the patient as part of a
medical system

Capability
- First Aid: Direct pressure, bandaging, basic stabilization
- EMT-Level Care: Advanced assessment, airway management, medication, and
clinical decision-making

Risk Consideration
- First Aid: Focus on doing no harm and preventing the situation from
worsening
- EMT-Level Care: Balances risk vs benefit under medical protocols

Operational Boundary
In this environment, we do not have:
- Medical direction
- Full equipment
- System support
- Controlled clinical conditions

Operational Takeaway
Stay within first aid scope:
- Control bleeding
- Stabilize the injury
- Transfer care

Just because you have the training to do more does not mean the environment
supports it. Acting beyond scope in the field can increase risk to both the
patient and the crew.

Addendum: Scope and Liability Awareness for EMS-Trained Personnel

Context
Many members of our workforce have EMT or paramedic training. That
experience is valuable, but in this environment we are operating under
workplace first aid expectations, not within an EMS system.

Legal Reality
When providing care in the field, actions are evaluated based on what is
reasonable for the environment and within expected scope.
- Basic first aid performed in good faith is generally protected
- Actions that go beyond first aid scope may be evaluated against a higher
standard
- Grossly negligent or unreasonable actions may not be protected under Good
Samaritan laws

Scope and Environment
EMS-level care assumes:
- Medical direction
- Established protocols
- Proper equipment
- Controlled clinical conditions

These elements are not present in most field work environments.

Operational Risk
Crossing scope in a workplace setting can:
- Increase risk to the injured person
- Introduce uncontrolled variables
- Create liability exposure for both the individual and the company
- Result in actions being judged outside workplace expectations

Decision Boundary
In this environment, the standard is:
- Identify the injury
- Control the problem (bleeding, airway, stabilization)
- Prevent the situation from worsening
- Transfer care to higher-level resources

Operational Takeaway
Scope is defined by the environment, not by individual training level.
Just because you have the knowledge to perform advanced care does not mean
the conditions support it.

Field Safety Reminder
Protect the patient by doing what is appropriate for the setting, not
everything you are trained to do.

Key Principle
Do what is necessary to control and stabilize. Avoid actions that introduce
additional risk without proper support or conditions.
