# Habituation to Warning Signals

**Date:** 2026-07-09

---

Topic: Habituation to Warning Signals

Situation

Field work produces a steady background of small warning signals. Equipment behaves a little differently than usual. A gauge or reading looks slightly off. A forecast trends the wrong way. Someone gets a nagging feeling that something isn't quite right. Most of the time, nothing comes of it.

Why It Matters

A signal that is followed by nothing bad happening, over and over, gradually stops functioning as a signal. This is habituation, and it is one of the main ways weak signals fail to do their job. Karl Weick and Kathleen Sutcliffe describe this directly in their work on high-reliability organizations. They found that the strongest organizations maintain a "sensitivity to operations," treating small anomalies as data worth a second look rather than background noise, precisely because repeated exposure tends to wear that sensitivity down over time.

This connects to drift as well. Sidney Dekker's account of drift describes how each individual decision to let something go can look locally reasonable, while the cumulative effect moves the baseline for what counts as normal. Habituation is drift with a memory. Nobody decides that the threshold for reacting should move. It moves anyway, one dismissed signal at a time, until the crew's working definition of "normal" has shifted without anyone noticing the shift happen.

Operational Hazards

- Equipment that "always does that" masks the one time the behavior is an actual developing fault, not a quirk.
- A radio call or briefing detail that is a little off gets waved through because it has been fine every other time.
- Conditions trending the wrong way get treated the same as day one, because the last several times the trend did not matter.
- A gut feeling of unease gets suppressed because raising it before did not lead anywhere.
- The more reliable a system, tool, or crew has been, the harder it becomes to notice the one time that reliability breaks down.

Control Measures

- Treat a repeated false alarm as information about the alarm itself, not as permission to stop noticing that category of signal.
- When something is dismissed, say why out loud instead of moving past it silently, so the reasoning behind the dismissal can be checked by someone else.
- Rotate who performs routine checks periodically. A fresh set of eyes has not been dulled by the same repetition.
- Separate "the signal happened again" from "the signal was actually checked and cleared." A real check resets the baseline. A quick dismissal does not.
- If the same signal keeps returning, treat the pattern itself as new information, even when each individual instance still looks minor on its own.

Sources / Supporting References

- Karl Weick and Kathleen Sutcliffe, Managing the Unexpected. High-reliability organizations maintain sensitivity to operations, treating weak signals as worth examining rather than filtering them out as noise.
- Sidney Dekker, Drift into Failure. The incremental normalization of what counts as acceptable is a defining feature of drift, and it applies to how a crew's threshold for reacting to a signal moves over time, not only to how a formal procedure erodes.
