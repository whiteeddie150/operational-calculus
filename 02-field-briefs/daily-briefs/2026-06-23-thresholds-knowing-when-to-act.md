MORNING SAFETY BRIEF

Topic: Thresholds, Knowing When to Act

Supporting PG&E Virtue: Owners

Situation

Every day in field work, we make decisions about when to continue, when to
pause, when to verify, and when to stop.

Most safety decisions are not made because one single thing happens.

More often, the decision to act comes from several smaller factors adding
up.

At some point, we reach a threshold.

That threshold is the moment when observation needs to become action.

Why It Matters

Experienced operators do not wait for certainty.

They look at the pattern.

A changing condition may not be enough by itself.

Fatigue may not be enough by itself.

A tool issue may not be enough by itself.

Weather may not be enough by itself.

But when several factors begin stacking together, the risk picture changes.

The question becomes:

Have I seen enough to act?

The Science

Gary Klein's work on Recognition-Primed Decision Making found that
experienced operators often make decisions by recognizing patterns and
mentally testing what is likely to happen next.

They do not always compare every possible option.

They ask:

• Does this situation make sense?
• Is it developing the way I expect?
• What happens next if nothing changes?
• Is action needed now?

Signal detection research also supports this idea. Operators constantly
balance two risks:

• Acting too early and creating unnecessary disruption
• Acting too late and losing the opportunity to influence the outcome

Good decision-making means recognizing when the cost of waiting has become
greater than the cost of acting.

Operational Threshold Matrix

When deciding whether to act, consider five factors:

1. Signal Strength

How strong is the evidence?

• Weak
• Moderate
• Strong

2. Trajectory

Where is this headed?

• Improving
• Stable
• Deteriorating

3. Consequence

If we are wrong, how bad could the outcome be?

• Minor
• Serious
• Severe

4. Reversibility

Can we undo the decision?

• Easily reversible
• Difficult to reverse
• Irreversible

5. Time Available

How long do we have before options shrink?

• Plenty of time
• Limited time
• Immediate action needed

Field Application

A single concern may not require action.

But several concerns together should get our attention.

Examples:

• Equipment is acting slightly different
• Crew is fatigued
• Weather is changing
• Access is getting worse
• The task is taking longer than expected
• Communication is becoming less clear

None of those may be an emergency by itself.

Together, they may mean the threshold has been crossed.

Questioning Attitude

PG&E's Questioning Attitude reminds us to prefer facts over assumptions.

A strong operator does not ignore discomfort, uncertainty, or changing
conditions.

They investigate them.

Ask:

• What changed?
• What am I assuming?
• Is this getting better or worse?
• What happens if I do nothing?
• Have we reached the point where action is needed?

Supporting PG&E Virtue

Owners

Owners do not wait for the situation to make the decision for them.

Owners recognize when conditions are changing and take action while options
still exist.

Safety Reminder

The best time to act is often before the situation becomes obvious to
everyone.

By the time the problem is undeniable, the window of influence may already
be smaller.

2-Sentence Safety Message

A threshold is the point where observation needs to become action. Pay
attention to signal strength, trajectory, consequence, reversibility, and
time because good decisions are often made before certainty arrives.

5 Quick Bullet Points

• Watch for factors stacking together
• Ask where the situation is headed
• Do not wait for certainty when consequences are serious
• Act while options still exist
• Use Questioning Attitude before assumptions become decisions

Closing Thought

Experience helps us recognize patterns. Ownership means acting when the
pattern tells us waiting is no longer the safest option.
