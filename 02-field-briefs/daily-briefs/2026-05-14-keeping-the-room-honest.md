Morning Safety Brief
Topic: Keeping the Room Honest – Good Operations Depend on Real Information

Situation
Most operational problems do not start because nobody noticed something was
wrong.

They usually start because:
- uncertainty stayed quiet
- assumptions went unchallenged
- concerns were softened
- people stopped speaking plainly about conditions

Good crews depend on accurate information flow, especially when conditions
start changing.

Why It Matters
Supervisors, crews, and operators can only make good decisions with real
information.

When people:
- hold back concerns
- avoid questioning the work
- assume someone else already knows
- stay quiet to avoid friction

situational awareness degrades and small problems become harder to catch
early.

Operational Risk
When honest communication breaks down:
- weak signals get missed
- assumptions spread
- verification weakens
- drift accelerates
- bad information starts driving decisions

Most of the time, the issue is not disagreement.

The issue is when people stop saying what they actually see.

Connections to Previous Briefs
We've talked before about:
- drift
- weak signals
- questioning attitude
- verification vs assumption
- interruption recovery
- operational reset

Keeping the room honest supports all of them.

The earlier concerns are spoken out loud:
- the easier drift is to catch
- the easier conditions are to reassess
- the easier problems are to correct before they compound

Where It Shows Up

In the Field
- pre-job briefings
- changing conditions
- unclear assignments
- hesitation to question the plan
- assumptions about controls

In the Office
- meetings where concerns stay quiet
- incomplete email communication
- rushed planning discussions
- avoiding difficult conversations
- silent disagreement

Behavioral Warning Signs
- "It's probably fine"
- quiet hesitation
- concerns getting softened
- nobody questioning assumptions
- agreement without verification
- uncertainty staying unspoken

Operational Strength
Strong crews:
- speak plainly
- challenge assumptions respectfully
- raise concerns early
- verify understanding
- keep information accurate even when uncomfortable

They do not pass uncertainty forward.

Connection to PG&E Standards
PG&E expects:
- questioning attitude
- verification over assumption
- stop when unsure

Those standards only work when people are willing to speak honestly about
what they are seeing.

Field Safety Reminder
Good operations depend on real information, not silent assumptions.

2-Sentence Safety Message
Most operational failures start as small concerns that never got fully
communicated. Keeping the room honest helps crews recognize and correct
problems before they compound.

5 Quick Bullet Points
- Speak plainly about conditions
- Question assumptions early
- Don't soften concerns
- Verify instead of assuming
- Real information protects crews

Annex: PG&E Doctrine Supporting Operational Honesty and Speaking Up

Context
This brief is aligned with existing PG&E Human Performance, SIF Prevention,
and operational learning doctrine.

The concepts discussed are not based on social or HR frameworks. They are
grounded in:
- operational reliability
- accurate information flow
- questioning attitude
- verification over assumption
- learning culture
- serious injury and fatality prevention

Key Doctrinal Alignments

1. Questioning Attitude
PG&E's SIF Prevention Field Guide emphasizes the importance of maintaining
a questioning attitude during work activities.

PG&E states:
"A questioning attitude promotes a preference for facts over assumptions
and opinions."

The guidance specifically warns against assumption-based thinking and
encourages employees to reassess conditions when uncertainty exists.

Operational Connection:
- challenge assumptions
- verify conditions
- speak honestly about uncertainty
- reassess changing situations

Reference:
https://www.pge.com/assets/pge/docs/about/doing-business-with-pge/SIF-Prevention-Field-Guide.pdf

---

2. Verification Over Assumption
PG&E doctrine repeatedly reinforces the idea that controls, conditions, and
understanding must be verified rather than assumed.

Operational Connection:
- silent assumptions degrade awareness
- incomplete information weakens decisions
- verification protects crews and operations

Core Principle:
Good operational decisions require accurate information.

---

3. Speak-Up Culture
PG&E safety reporting and CPUC safety materials reference the importance of
maintaining a "Speak-Up Culture."

PG&E states:
"The Safety Observations Program plays a critical role… reinforcing
positive work practices, and driving PG&E's Speak-Up culture."

Operational Connection:
- raising concerns early
- reporting hazards honestly
- communicating operational reality
- preventing small problems from remaining hidden

---

4. Human and Organizational Performance (HOP)
PG&E's PSEMS / SIF Capacity & Learning Playbook emphasizes operational
learning and honest discussion of mistakes, shortcuts, and near-hits.

PG&E states:
"Adopting a HOP mindset… will result in cultivating a true speak-up culture
where mistakes, shortcuts, and near-hits are freely shared…"

Operational Connection:
- discussing operational concerns openly
- learning instead of hiding problems
- surfacing drift early
- improving operational awareness

---

5. Drift and Operational Reality
PG&E doctrine recognizes that work conditions evolve and drift over time.

PG&E states:
"Drift is normal."

The playbook also acknowledges that:
"Work is complex and dynamic, requiring those who perform and touch the
work to improvise in real-time."

Operational Connection:
- changing conditions require reassessment
- silent uncertainty increases operational risk
- accurate communication helps crews recognize degradation early

---

Operational Summary

Across PG&E doctrine, the themes are consistent:
- question assumptions
- verify conditions
- communicate honestly
- raise concerns early
- maintain operational awareness
- support learning over silence

Field Application

Good operations depend on:
- accurate information flow
- honest communication
- willingness to question the work
- identifying uncertainty before problems compound

Key Principle

Good operations depend on real information, not silent assumptions.
