Morning Safety Brief
Topic: Cumulative Fatigue – When Repetition Turns Into Autopilot

Situation
Not all fatigue looks like exhaustion.

Some of the most dangerous fatigue develops slowly through:
- repetitive work
- sustained attention
- repeated checks
- routine exposure
- continuous low-level workload

Over time, attention can quietly shift from active observation into
autopilot.

Why It Matters
Research in human performance and operational psychology shows that
repetitive tasks gradually reduce:
- vigilance
- error detection
- active verification
- situational awareness

The more familiar the work becomes, the easier it is to assume instead of
observe.

Operational Risk
Cumulative fatigue often develops unnoticed because:
- the work still feels routine
- the task feels familiar
- nothing bad has happened yet
- repetition creates comfort

Eventually:
- checks become automatic
- subtle changes get missed
- assumptions replace verification
- weak signals stop standing out

The danger is not just being tired.

The danger is becoming automatic.

Connections to Previous Briefs
We've talked before about:
- drift
- verification vs assumption
- weak signals
- interruption recovery
- questioning attitude
- normalization of deviance

Cumulative fatigue creates conditions where all of those problems become
more likely.

The more repetitive the task becomes:
- the easier drift develops
- the harder weak signals are to recognize
- the more likely verification gets replaced by expectation

Where It Shows Up

In the Field
- repetitive switching or inspections
- repeated equipment checks
- driving long distances
- repetitive paperwork or documentation
- routine operational tasks

In the Office
- repetitive email processing
- continuous screen work
- repetitive meetings
- sustained multitasking
- prolonged low-level workload

Behavioral Warning Signs
- "going through the motions"
- checks becoming automatic
- increased assumption
- missing small changes
- reduced curiosity
- operating on routine instead of observation

Operational Strength
Strong crews recognize when repetition is degrading awareness:
- slow down periodically
- deliberately re-verify
- reset attention
- rotate focus when possible
- resist autopilot thinking

They stay engaged with the task instead of predicting it.

Connection to PG&E Standards
PG&E expects:
- questioning attitude
- verification over assumption
- stop when unsure

Cumulative fatigue directly challenges all three.

Field Safety Reminder
The more repetitive the task becomes, the more deliberate verification
needs to become.

2-Sentence Safety Message
Fatigue does not always look exhausted. Sometimes it looks comfortable,
automatic, and overly familiar with the work.

5 Quick Bullet Points
- Repetition reduces vigilance
- Familiarity increases assumption
- Autopilot weakens verification
- Slow down periodically and reset attention
- Stay engaged with the task, not the expectation
