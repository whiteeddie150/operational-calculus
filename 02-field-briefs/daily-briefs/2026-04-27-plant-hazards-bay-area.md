Morning Safety Brief
Topic: Plant Hazards – Bay Area Field Exposure

Situation
Not all hazards are mechanical. In the Bay Area, crews routinely work
around vegetation that can cause skin reactions, burns, or injury. Many
exposures happen during routine work like clearing, walking access paths,
or handling tools.

Why It Matters
Plant exposures are often unexpected and can escalate quickly if not
recognized and managed early. Oils, sap, and plant fibers can transfer to
tools, gloves, and clothing, spreading the exposure beyond the initial
contact.
Early recognition and simple first aid can reduce severity and prevent
complications.

Common Hazardous Plants (Bay Area)

Poison Oak (Primary Exposure Risk)
- Leaves of three, can be green or red
- Found in most field environments
- Causes skin reaction from urushiol oil
- Oil spreads easily from tools, clothing, and skin

Stinging Nettle
- Found in moist, shaded areas
- Fine hairs inject irritants on contact
- Causes burning, itching, and redness

Giant Hogweed (Rare, High Consequence)
- Large plant with umbrella-shaped white flowers
- Sap reacts with sunlight causing severe skin burns
- Can result in blistering and long-term sensitivity

Oleander (Urban/Facility Hazard)
- Common in landscaping and roadways
- Highly toxic if ingested
- Smoke from burning is hazardous

Foxtail Grass (Indirect Injury Hazard)
- Dry grass with barbed seeds
- Seeds embed in skin, socks, or eyes
- Can cause irritation or infection

Operational Hazards
- Unrecognized plant contact
- Oil/sap transfer from tools and gloves
- Working through vegetation without awareness
- Delayed recognition of exposure
- Spreading contamination to other crew members

Control Measures
- Identify vegetation before starting work
- Avoid direct contact with unknown plants
- Use gloves, sleeves, and appropriate PPE
- Clean tools and gloves after exposure
- Wash exposed skin as soon as possible

Immediate First Aid (Within Scope)

Poison Oak
- Wash exposed skin with soap and water as soon as possible
- Avoid spreading oil to other areas
- Use cool compresses to reduce irritation
- Seek medical care for severe reactions

Stinging Nettle
- Do not rub the area
- Rinse with water and gently clean
- Use cool compresses
- Symptoms typically resolve with time

Giant Hogweed
- Avoid sunlight on exposed area immediately
- Wash skin thoroughly
- Cover the area to block sunlight
- Seek medical evaluation for any reaction

Oleander
- Do not ingest any part of the plant
- Wash hands after contact
- Seek immediate medical care if ingestion is suspected

Foxtail Grass
- Remove visible seeds carefully
- Do not push deeper into skin or eyes
- Flush eyes with clean water if exposed
- Seek medical care if embedded or causing irritation

What NOT to Do
- Do not ignore exposure symptoms
- Do not spread contamination through clothing or tools
- Do not attempt advanced treatment beyond first aid
- Do not continue work if exposure is uncontrolled

Stop Work and Control
If vegetation exposure cannot be avoided or controlled, the condition is
unsafe.
Stop work and reassess before continuing.

Field Safety Reminder
Out here, some hazards don't make noise. You have to see them before they
become a problem.

2-Sentence Safety Message
Plant hazards are common and often overlooked. Identify them early, avoid
contact, and treat exposures immediately.

5 Quick Bullet Points
- Know what's growing around you
- Avoid contact with unknown plants
- Wash exposure early
- Don't spread contamination
- Stop work if exposure can't be controlled
