
# Friday safety

**Sent:** 2026-05-22

---

**Team,**

**Friday Safety Brief**
**Topic: Leaving the Operation Squared Away**

---

**Situation**

By Friday, most crews are carrying some level of:
- Fatigue
- Repetition
- Workload accumulation
- Mental transition toward the weekend

That is normal.

The challenge is maintaining operational discipline and professionalism all
the way through the end of the operational period.

---

**Why It Matters**

Strong operations depend on more than just completing the work.

They depend on:
- Clean communication
- Verified status
- Accurate pass-downs
- Equipment that is ready and accounted for
- Clear operational understanding

A clean operation is easier to operate safely.

---

**Operational Risk**

When people mentally transition out of the operation before the work is
actually complete:

- Communication shortens
- Assumptions increase
- Details get missed
- Unresolved issues get carried forward
- Situational awareness degrades

Most of the time, these are not major failures.

They are small unresolved issues that quietly follow the next crew or
operational period forward.

---

**Connections to Previous Briefs**

We have talked before about:
- Drift
- Weak signals
- Verification vs. assumption
- Operational reset
- Crew cross-checking
- Tempo vs. awareness

Leaving the operation in good order helps reduce all of them.

Disciplined crews:
- Communicate clearly
- Clean up operational loose ends
- Verify status
- Clarify assumptions
- Help the next operational period start from a position of awareness
instead of uncertainty

---

**Where It Shows Up**

These patterns appear in every work environment, whether you are in the
field or coordinating from the office. Both matter equally because both
feed the next operational period.

**In the Field**
- Equipment accountability
- Clear vehicle and equipment status
- Operational cleanup
- Complete communication of hazards or changing conditions
- Ensuring controls are still understood

**In the Office / Coordination Environment**
- Accurate pass-downs
- Closing communication loops
- Updating operational information
- Clarifying unresolved issues
- Avoiding assumption-based turnover

---

**Behavioral Warning Signs**
- "We'll deal with it Monday"
- Rushing closeout
- Shortened communication
- Incomplete pass-downs
- Mental checkout before operational completion
- Assuming someone else already handled it

---

**Operational Strength**

Experienced crews:
- Finish controlled
- Communicate clearly
- Close operational loops
- Leave equipment and information ready for the next shift
- Support the next operational period

Professional crews do not just complete the work.

They leave the operation ready for what comes next.

---

**Connection to PG&E Standards and Doctrine**

Two standards connect most directly to this brief:

**Questioning attitude and verification over assumption** are both
undermined when crews rush turnover or rely on what they think is true
rather than what they have confirmed. A hurried pass-down is one of the
most common places where this breaks down.

**SIF prevention and operational learning** depend on accurate information
flowing between crews and operational periods. Gaps in that flow are where
small unresolved issues become the starting conditions for the next event.

Strong operational continuity depends on disciplined turnover, verified
conditions, and information that is current when the next crew needs it.

---

**Field Safety Reminder**

The crew coming in next is starting from wherever you left it. Leave them
in a good position.

---

**2-Sentence Safety Message**

Fatigue and familiarity tend to accumulate quietly by the end of the week.
Disciplined crews maintain their standard through the finish and leave the
operation clearer, cleaner, and safer for whoever comes next.

---

**5 Quick Bullet Points**
- Finish controlled, not rushed
- Close communication loops
- Verify instead of assuming
- Leave equipment and information ready
- Support the next operational period
