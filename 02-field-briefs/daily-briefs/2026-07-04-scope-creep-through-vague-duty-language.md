# Scope Creep Through Vague Duty Language

**Status:** Drafted for delivery (queued topic, see CLAUDE.md)
**Date:** 2026-07-04

---

Topic: Scope Creep Through Vague Duty Language

Situation

Most role descriptions include a catch-all line. Some version of "other duties as assigned" or "and related tasks as needed" appears in nearly every job description. That language exists for legitimate reasons. No written description can anticipate every situation a role will encounter.

Over time, real tasks get added to a role through that catch-all clause. Individually, each addition seems reasonable. Collectively, they can move the role a long way from what it was originally trained, equipped, and assessed for.

Why It Matters

This is drift. It is the same mechanism Sidney Dekker describes in Drift into Failure. Systems move away from their original safety margins through a long series of small, locally reasonable adaptations, not through one deliberate decision. Applied here, the adaptation shows up in duty language rather than field procedure, but the mechanism is the same. Nothing about any single addition looks like a problem, and the written role is never formally revisited to catch up with what the role has actually become.

The gap between the written role and the real role does not announce itself. It accumulates quietly, one "just this once" at a time.

Operational Risk

- Tasks get added informally, without updating the position's training or hazard assessment.
- The assumption that existing training "basically covers" the new task, without checking.
- No single addition feels significant enough to trigger a formal review.
- The person absorbing the expanded scope often doesn't flag it. Declining a reasonable-sounding individual request feels unreasonable in the moment.
- By the time the gap between written role and actual role is visible to anyone else, it has usually existed for a long time.

Control Measures

- Periodically ask, in plain terms, what you are actually being asked to do that isn't written into your role.
- Treat a pattern of "one-off" requests as a signal worth naming, not a series of unrelated events.
- Push accumulated changes to a real, formal review rather than letting them stay informal indefinitely.
- Leads and supervisors should audit what a role is actually doing against what it's trained and assessed for on a fixed schedule, not only after something goes wrong.
- Use the catch-all clause for what it's meant for. It provides occasional flexibility. It is not a permanent substitute for redefining the job.

Field Safety Reminder

"Other duties as assigned" is meant to cover the exception. It is not supposed to quietly become the job description.

2-Sentence Safety Message

A role's real risk exposure can expand far past its written description through nothing more than a series of small, reasonable-sounding requests. If nobody periodically checks the actual work against the written role, that gap grows invisibly until something forces it into view.

5 Quick Bullet Points

- Catch-all duty language is meant for exceptions, not routine expansion.
- Small additions to a role rarely trigger a formal safety review on their own.
- Ask periodically what you're actually doing versus what's written down.
- A pattern of "just this once" is itself a signal worth naming.
- Push accumulated scope changes to a real review. Don't let them stay informal.

Supporting References

This brief applies Sidney Dekker's concept of drift (Drift into Failure) to administrative and role-scope adaptation rather than field procedure. The underlying claim is his. Meaningful departure from a defined baseline rarely happens through a single decision. It happens through an accumulation of small, individually defensible steps that are never added up and checked against the original baseline.
