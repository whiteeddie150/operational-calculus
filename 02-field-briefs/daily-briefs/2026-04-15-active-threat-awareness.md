Morning Safety Brief
Topic: Active Threat Awareness – Get Out, Hide Out, Take Out

Situation
Active threat events are low-frequency but high-consequence incidents that
can occur in everyday work environments. These events develop quickly and
often conclude within minutes, sometimes before law enforcement arrives.

Why It Matters
Federal data shows most active threat events are over in less than five
minutes and often end before law enforcement intervention. Outcomes are
heavily influenced by early recognition and immediate action (Federal
Bureau of Investigation [FBI]; Department of Homeland Security [DHS]).
In these situations, time, space, and options are lost quickly, and delayed
decisions reduce available choices.

Operational Hazards
- Delay in recognizing the event (normalcy bias)
- Freezing or waiting for more information
- Moving toward familiar exits instead of safer options
- Misjudging direction due to sound distortion
- Waiting for group direction instead of acting

Control Measures (Get Out, Hide Out, Take Out)
- Get Out: Leave the area immediately if a safe path exists
- Hide Out: Use barriers, lock or block access, and stay out of view
- Take Out: As a last resort, act to stop the threat if necessary to
protect life
- Make early decisions, do not wait for perfect information
- Maintain awareness of exits and surroundings at all times

Key Insights (Lesser Known but Critical)
- Most people hesitate or freeze initially, early action reduces exposure
- Events move faster than expected, often over in minutes
- Sound can be misleading, do not rely on it alone for direction
- Even small barriers and distance significantly increase safety
- Communication may fail or become unreliable during the event

Common Active Threat Parameters

- Most attackers are male and act alone
- Typically a single individual, not a coordinated group
- Often motivated by a personal grievance tied to the location
- Handguns are the most commonly used weapon
- Events are fast-moving and dynamic, not static
- Attackers may move through areas rather than stay in one place
- Most incidents last only a few minutes, often 2–5 minutes
- Many events end before law enforcement arrives
- Occur in everyday environments like workplaces, public areas, and
commercial spaces
- Little to no warning for those immediately affected

Key Takeaways

- It happens fast
- It happens in normal places
- It's usually one person
- There is little warning
- Early action matters more than anything
