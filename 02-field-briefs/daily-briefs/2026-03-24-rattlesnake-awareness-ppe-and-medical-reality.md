them, stepping over something or reaching into brush. Watch your footing,
keep your hands visible, and use your PG&E gaiters in high-risk areas. They

Tailboard Safety Briefing

Topic: Rattlesnake Awareness, PPE, and Medical Reality

---

Situation

Crews working in vegetation, rocky terrain, and remote areas may encounter
rattlesnakes, especially in warmer conditions. Snakes rely on camouflage
and are often not seen until disturbed.

---

Why It Matters

Most rattlesnake bites occur to the lower extremities and hands when
workers step near or reach into concealed areas. Proper awareness and PPE,
including snake gaiters provided by PG&E, significantly reduce the
likelihood and severity of a bite.

---

Operational Hazards

• Placing hands into brush, holes, or under equipment
• Stepping over logs or rocks without visibility
• Working in tall grass or low-visibility terrain
• Moving debris or materials without checking underneath
• Attempting to move or handle a snake

---

Control Measures

• Watch your step and scan continuously
• Never place hands where you cannot see
• Step on top of obstacles, not over them
• Wear PG&E-issued snake gaiters in high-risk areas
• Give snakes space, do not approach or attempt to move them

---

Relevant Federal Safety Guidance

The CDC advises avoiding contact with snakes, maintaining awareness in
snake habitats, and seeking immediate medical care if bitten.
NIOSH emphasizes hazard avoidance and PPE use as primary controls in
outdoor work environments.

---

Supporting Language from PG&E Governing Documents

PG&E emphasizes that safety is the highest priority and that employees are
responsible for recognizing hazards and protecting themselves and others.

---

Supporting Language from IBEW 1245 Physical Agreement

The Company and Union affirm that maintaining safe working conditions and
preventing injury is a shared responsibility.

---

Field Safety Reminder

Your best defense is awareness and distance. PPE like gaiters helps, but
the goal is to never get bitten.

---

Medical Reality (For Awareness Only)

Typical Antivenom Treatment (Hospital-Based)

- The primary treatment in the U.S. is CroFab (Crotalidae Polyvalent
Immune Fab) or ANAVIP
- Initial dosing is typically 4–6 vials IV, followed by reassessment
- Additional doses are given based on swelling progression, systemic
symptoms, and labs
- Treatment is only administered in a hospital setting, not in the field

Key Point for Crews

You are not treating the bite, you are managing the patient and getting
them to definitive care quickly.

---

Shelf Life of Antivenom

- CroFab typically has a shelf life of ~2–3 years when stored properly
- Requires controlled storage conditions (hospital/pharmacy level)
- Not carried in field operations due to storage, cost, and administration
requirements

---

Field First Aid Priorities (High-Level)

• Move the patient away from the snake
• Keep the patient calm and limit movement
• Immobilize the affected limb at heart level
• Remove constrictive items (rings, watches, boots if needed)
• Activate EMS and transport rapidly

Do NOT:
• Cut the wound
• Apply suction
• Use a tourniquet
• Apply ice

---

2-Sentence Safety Message

Rattlesnakes are hard to see and most bites happen to the hands and feet
when we get too close. Stay alert, wear your gaiters in high-risk areas,
and give snakes space.

---

5 Quick Bullet Points

• Watch where you step and place your hands
• Wear PG&E-issued snake gaiters in brush areas
• Never reach into unseen spaces
• Do not attempt to move or handle snakes
• If bitten, stay calm and get to advanced care immediately
