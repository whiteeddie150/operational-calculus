Fire Season Operational Awareness Brief – Part 1
Topic: Tempo vs Awareness – Maintaining Operational Control During Fire
Season

Situation
As fire-season operations increase, operational tempo naturally increases
with them.

Campaign-fire environments introduce:
- rapidly changing conditions
- compressed timelines
- evolving priorities
- increased radio traffic
- fatigue accumulation
- environmental exposure
- overlapping operational demands

None of that is unusual. It is part of operating around dynamic fire
environments.

The challenge is maintaining awareness and disciplined controls as the pace
of operations increases.

Why It Matters
Research in aviation, wildfire operations, human factors, and
high-reliability organizations consistently shows that situational
awareness degrades as workload, fatigue, and operational tempo increase.

When tempo starts outrunning awareness:
- communication shortens
- assumptions increase
- verification weakens
- weak signals get missed
- drift accelerates

The operation usually does not fail all at once.

It starts when the pace of the work begins moving faster than people's
awareness of it.

Operational Reality
Fire-season operations are inherently dynamic.

Conditions change:
- weather shifts
- priorities move
- communications compress
- hazards evolve
- information becomes incomplete

Strong crews recognize that increased tempo is expected.

Losing operational awareness is not.

Connections to Previous Briefs
We've talked before about:
- drift
- weak signals
- verification vs assumption
- interruption recovery
- cumulative fatigue
- autopilot
- crew cross-checking

Campaign-fire environments amplify all of them.

As operational tempo rises:
- fatigue accumulates
- assumptions spread faster
- communication quality degrades
- attention narrows
- crews become more vulnerable to autopilot behavior

Where It Shows Up

In the Field
- rushing transitions
- shortened communication
- assumption-based decisions
- missed environmental changes
- incomplete reassessment of controls
- multitasking under radio and operational pressure

In the Office / Coordination Environment
- compressed planning cycles
- fragmented situational awareness
- rapid task switching
- overloaded communication channels
- assumption carryover between operational periods

Behavioral Warning Signs
- "We don't have time"
- shortened conversations
- reduced questioning
- skipping verification
- increased frustration
- scattered attention
- rushing familiar tasks

Operational Strength
Strong crews deliberately protect awareness during high-tempo operations:
- slow down enough to verify
- reassess changing conditions
- communicate clearly
- challenge assumptions early
- use the crew as a second set of eyes
- maintain control instead of reacting to tempo

Fast is not the same thing as controlled.

Connection to PG&E Standards and Doctrine

PG&E's SIF Prevention and Human Performance doctrine emphasizes:
- questioning attitude
- verification over assumption
- operational learning
- stop when unsure
- maintaining essential controls

PG&E's SIF Prevention Field Guide states:
"A questioning attitude promotes a preference for facts over assumptions
and opinions."

PG&E's PSEMS / Capacity & Learning doctrine also recognizes:
"Drift is normal."

And:
"Work is complex and dynamic, requiring those who perform and touch the
work to improvise in real-time."

These concepts become even more important during fire-season operations
where tempo, workload, and environmental complexity increase simultaneously.

Field Safety Reminder
The environment will naturally push operations faster during fire season.
Strong crews deliberately protect awareness as tempo increases.

2-Sentence Safety Message
Campaign-fire operations naturally increase workload, pace, and complexity.
Maintaining awareness, verification, and communication under tempo is one
of the strongest operational controls we have.

5 Quick Bullet Points
- Tempo naturally rises during fire season
- Fast is not the same thing as controlled
- Verify even when operations accelerate
- Protect awareness as workload increases
- Strong crews control the pace instead of reacting to it

Operational Risk – Information Lag

Campaign-fire environments change quickly:
- fire behavior evolves
- operational priorities shift
- access conditions change
- hazards move faster than reporting cycles

One of the hidden risks during dynamic operations is information lag:
- operating from outdated assumptions
- relying on stale situational awareness
- believing the operational picture is more current than it actually is

Information can be technically correct and still become operationally wrong
if conditions have already changed.

As tempo increases:
- reassessment frequency drops
- communications compress
- people trust prior information longer
- assumptions begin replacing active verification

Strong crews recognize that situational awareness has a shelf life during
fast-moving operations.

Operational Reminder
Conditions should be continuously reassessed, especially when operating
near campaign-fire environments where field reality may already be evolving
ahead of the last update.
