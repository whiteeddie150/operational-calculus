Morning Safety Brief
Topic: Heat Illness – Early Season Risk

Situation
We're entering warmer conditions, and early-season heat events are where
crews are most vulnerable. The body is not yet acclimatized, and routine
work can quickly turn into a medical issue.

Why It Matters
Heat illness is a leading cause of weather-related occupational fatalities
in the United States. NIOSH notes that most serious cases occur during the
first few days of working in hot conditions before the body adapts.
What feels like "just another warm day" is where incidents start.

Operational Hazards
- New or returning workers not acclimatized
- Heavy work in direct sun or low wind
- Dehydration from inadequate fluid intake
- Ignoring early signs like fatigue, cramps, or dizziness
- Pushing through symptoms instead of stopping

Control Measures
- Gradually build up workload over several days
- Hydrate consistently, not just when thirsty
- Take breaks in shaded or cooler areas
- Watch for early signs in yourself and others
- Speak up and stop work at the first signs of heat stress

Relevant Federal Safety Guidance
NIOSH emphasizes acclimatization, hydration, and early recognition as key
controls for preventing heat-related illness.
https://www.cdc.gov/niosh/topics/heatstress

Field Safety Reminder
Heat illness doesn't start with collapse, it starts with small signs. Catch
it early.

2-Sentence Safety Message
Early-season heat is when crews are most at risk because the body hasn't
adapted yet. Hydrate, take breaks, and stop work at the first sign of heat
stress.

5 Quick Bullet Points
