# Margin, Adaptability, and Resilience

## Origin

This framework began with an observation from commercial real estate. Once a contract is signed, it begins to decay. Conditions shift, assumptions age, and the agreement that made sense the day it was signed slowly stops matching the world it was written for.

The same pattern shows up anywhere a decision maker holds a set of live options that narrows over time. A quarterback has roughly six seconds after the snap before his options collapse to almost nothing. A crew operating near an active fire holds a set of viable choices that narrows as conditions change. In every case, something usable at the start becomes less usable simply by the passage of time, before anything has gone wrong.

## Margin

Margin is the measure of live options available to a decision maker at a given moment.

Margin declines within a bounded decision cycle. Once a cycle begins, whether that cycle is a single play, a single incident, or a single shift, margin does not recover on its own. It can reset between cycles, but that reset needs to be examined closely rather than assumed. A reset earned through the agent's own effort, a new set of downs gained by good play, is a different kind of event than a reset that simply happens to the agent, a lucky break, an opponent's error, a shift in outside conditions. Both are real. They are not the same thing, and a full account of margin should say which kind occurred.

Margin does not need an outside authority to be real. It belongs to the person in the situation the way reaction time does. What margin does need, if it is going to be compared across people or teams, is a shared reference point recognized by others skilled in the same domain. Without that, two people in an identical situation could report entirely different margins with no way to judge between them.

## Adaptability

Adaptability is the continuous act of recalculating which options remain live within existing margin, and adjusting behavior to match.

It operates the entire time margin exists, from the widest point to the narrowest. It does not require anything to have failed yet. A quarterback reading a shifting coverage, a crew adjusting tactics as new information arrives, these are adaptability at work while the situation is still being managed rather than endured.

## Resilience

Resilience is the capacity to continue functioning once available options have been reduced to the point where further adaptation offers little additional influence.

The boundary between adaptability and resilience is not a fixed threshold. It should be drawn relative to the specific circumstance and the outcome being sought, not treated as identical across every situation. This point deserves more scrutiny than the framework has given it so far. Erik Hollnagel's work on resilience engineering treats anticipation and response as part of one continuous capacity rather than two stages separated by a clean line. A system that anticipates well may never experience a sharp moment where options fall to zero, since good anticipation keeps expanding the response range before anything close to collapse becomes necessary. The clean line between adaptability and resilience may owe more to the football metaphor, where a play has a definite end, than to how these things actually unfold in real operations, which rarely stop the way a play does.

## An Unproven Claim Worth Naming

One appealing but unverified idea sits underneath this framework: that using margin efficiently on one occasion conserves capacity for the next. This has not been demonstrated, only suggested by analogy. Whether disciplined use of margin in one incident actually improves a crew's position in the next has not been tested against real cases. It should be treated as a hypothesis, not a settled part of the framework, until it is.

## How This Framework Should Be Used

This is not a case study tool. Applying it to one dramatic historical event risks the same failure that happened to Weick's Mann Gulch paper: the event calcifies into the fixed reference point, and people start remembering the story instead of running the framework itself.

Use this instead as a functional prism. Take any real situation, strip it down to its bare facts, what closed, what was tried, what happened, without the narrative texture that makes an event memorable. Feed that stripped account through the three concepts. Where did margin actually sit. What was adaptability doing while margin still existed. Where would resilience have needed to take over if margin had closed further than it did. The output is the value, not the input. Ten different situations run through this same method should produce ten different readings, and none of those ten should become the one everyone remembers instead of the method itself.

## Disciplines for Keeping This Honest

Keep the three concepts separated. The recurring temptation is to let two of them blur together, especially adaptability and resilience. Every time that happens, pull them back apart.

Test the split against real situations, not just vocabulary. Applying this only has value if the three way split changes a conclusion a team would otherwise reach. If it only renames what everyone already understood, it has added nothing.

Hold scrutiny at the level a question was actually asked. Do not escape upward into a broader frame to avoid a specific, pointed challenge.

Treat every claim here as falsifiable, including the ones in this document. The unproven conservation claim above and the adaptability and resilience boundary are both flagged as open, not settled, on purpose.
