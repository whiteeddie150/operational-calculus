# Delivery Structure

Standing reference for how daily safety briefs are written and archived going forward. This governs process, not content — see `02-field-briefs/THEME_ANALYSIS.md` for what the first 65 briefs actually said when checked against the literature, and `03-case-studies/` for developed comparisons against specific sources.

## 1. Weekly rhythm (Monday–Thursday)

Monday through Thursday follow the standard tailboard format already established across the existing 65-brief archive:

- Situation
- Why It Matters
- Operational Hazards
- Control Measures
- Sources / Supporting References

Drift and weak signals are the throughline concept for this rhythm. They were the two most recurring themes in the existing archive — drift by name or clear reference in 18+ of 65 briefs, weak signals in 11 — which is the evidence for treating them as the spine rather than one topic among many (see THEME_ANALYSIS.md for the count).

Fatigue, decision thresholds, recovery-after-injury, and PPE-as-behavior are real, recurring themes in their own right, but none of them is a second throughline. Write them as instances of drift (small things going uncorrected until they aren't small anymore) or weak signals (the job telling you something before it becomes obvious) showing up in a specific hazard — not as stand-alone topics disconnected from that spine. Name the hazard, then show which of the two it's an example of.

## 2. Every second or third Friday breaks format on purpose

On a roughly biweekly-to-triweekly cadence, Friday does not follow the Monday–Thursday shape. It alternates between two types.

**Deep dive.** Take one real, documented historical case — Rattlesnake Fire, Mann Gulch, or another actual incident — and slow down to examine it properly: what happened, in what order, what the people involved actually knew and when, and what specifically went wrong. State plainly, in the brief itself, that this is an illustration of the drift/weak-signals throughline and not a new center of the work. Hold it to the standard set in `03-case-studies/rattlesnake-fire-communication-lag.md`: read the actual primary source, compare it honestly against what the briefs already argue, and say plainly where the parallel holds and where it doesn't. Do not inflate the comparison to make the archive look more original than it is.

**Hand back to the room.** State one real claim from the research, plainly, in a sentence or two. Follow it with a direct question asking whether it matches the room's actual field experience. Nothing else — no Situation/Hazards/Controls structure, no bullet points, no closing thought. Just the claim and the question. The purpose is to test whether the research is actually true of this crew's work, not to teach at them. Record what comes back the same day in `06-field-notes/` (see that folder's README).

## 3. Attribution rule (standing, not occasional)

Any concept drawn from a named researcher — Weick, Klein, Dekker, Conklin, Graham, Kegan and Lahey, Boyd, or anyone else — gets that person named directly in the brief, the same way the briefs from April 2026 onward already do (Klein cited by name four times, Weick twice, Dekker three times in the existing archive — see THEME_ANALYSIS.md). This applies to every brief, not just deep dives.

This rule exists because it was already violated and had to be fixed after the fact: `2026-01-23-fatigue-and-risk.md` and `2026-02-19-reduced-visibility-rain-and-icy-road-conditions.md` both echoed Gordon Graham's "predictable is preventable" line closely enough to need rewording, and neither originally credited him. If a brief leans on someone's framework — Dekker's account of drift, Graham's predictable/preventable framing, Klein's recognition-primed decisions, whoever it is — name them in that brief, not just in a later analysis document.

## 4. Format is not sacred

The standard tailboard shape (Situation / Why It Matters / Hazards / Controls / Sources) is a default, not a ritual. Varying length, structure, or delivery style occasionally — a shorter message, a different order, a brief with no bullet points at all — is intentional and encouraged. The goal is a crew that is still reading and thinking, not one that has learned to skim a familiar template. If the format calcifies into something people stop noticing, it has stopped doing its job, and it should be broken on purpose more often, not less.
