## Project Scope

This project develops safety and operational-awareness content for high-consequence field work: fire, EMS, and utility operations. It is not a general theory of human participation in systems.

Current status is recorded in `00-Governance/PROJECT_STATUS.md`. Check that file for the latest state before assuming anything about where the project stands.

## Archive

65 real daily safety briefs, recovered from Gmail, spanning January through July 2026, live in `02-field-briefs/daily-briefs/`, indexed with a README.

Standalone reference material — proven templates and annexes — lives directly in `02-field-briefs/`, also indexed with its own README.

## Delivery Structure

Daily briefs Monday through Thursday follow the standard tailboard format proven across the archive.

Drift and weak signals are the throughline concept, since they were the two most recurring themes found in the existing material.

Every second or third Friday breaks format on purpose, alternating between two types:
- A deep dive into a real historical case (such as Rattlesnake Fire or Mann Gulch).
- A hand-back-to-the-room session, where a single research claim is stated plainly and the audience is asked whether it matches their actual field experience.

Full detail is in `00-Governance/DELIVERY_STRUCTURE.md`.

## Attribution Rule

Any concept drawn from a named researcher — including Weick, Klein, Dekker, Conklin, Graham, Boyd, or Kegan and Lahey — gets that person credited directly in the brief. This is a standing requirement, not an occasional flourish.

## Content Guidance for Sensitive Material

Some topics originate from the author's direct professional experience inside a specific current employer, including observed organizational and safety practice concerns.

When writing briefs that draw on this kind of material:
- Write at the level of the general pattern only.
- Never name the specific employer, team, role, individuals, or incidents involved.
- The pattern is the teaching content. The specific situation is private context that informs the author's authority to teach it, not something to be written down or published.
- If asked to draft a brief on a sensitive topic, check with the user before including any detail that could identify a specific organization, incident, or person, even indirectly.
